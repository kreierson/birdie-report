Friday at **Royal Birkdale** somehow gave us two kinds of golf chaos at once.

First, **Lucas Herbert** and **Sam Burns** both shot **62**, tying the lowest round ever recorded in a men's major.

Then the leaderboard got redrawn anyway.

According to **Associated Press reporting published Friday, July 17, 2026**, and **The Open's official round-two coverage and leaderboard**, checked on **Saturday, July 18, 2026**, Herbert leads **The 154th Open** at **8-under 132** after his record-tying round. The official Open recap says **Jackson Suber**, **Cameron Young**, and **Ryan Gerard** are two back at **6-under**, while **Burns**, **Si Woo Kim**, and **Bryson DeChambeau** are at **5-under** after DeChambeau was assessed a late **two-stroke penalty under Rule 8.1**.

That is not a normal halfway board.

It is much better than normal.

This piece is based on **AP's July 17, 2026 round-two report** and **The Open's official July 17 leaderboard and round-two coverage**, all checked on **Saturday, July 18, 2026**. No pretending I was camped behind the scoring trailer with a referee headset and a flask full of links-golf wisdom.

For the current **Birkdale** cluster first, read [our Round 1 story on Jackson Suber's surprise lead](/blog/jackson-suber-open-round-1-lead-birkdale-2026/), [the opening-day conditions piece](/blog/royal-birkdale-open-starts-baked-out-2026/), and [our Friday opinion on why the debutants crashing the script was good for the championship](/blog/jackson-suber-open-debutants-good-opinion-2026/).

## Herbert Turned Friday Morning Into a Real Open Story

Herbert's round was not some sleepy little **66** that happened to grab the lead because everybody else stalled out.

It was a full-blown major-history sprint.

AP reported that Herbert birdied his first **three holes**, got to **8-under through 12**, and missed a **5-foot par putt on 18** that would have made him the first man to shoot **61** in a major. The Open's official recap adds that he matched the championship's lowest front nine with **28**, previously reached at **Royal Birkdale** in **1983**, and got to **9-under for the round** with a birdie at **16** before the finish finally got awkward.

That awkward finish matters because it keeps the round human.

He still had to scramble.
He still had to save par at **17**.
He still had to deal with the very stupid emotional burden of realizing you might be about to do something nobody in men's major golf has done before.

He did not get the **61**.
He still got the lead.

That is a fair trade.

## Burns Made the Back Nine Completely Ridiculous

The second historic number somehow felt even stranger because it came from a player who was not even sure he would make the trip.

AP reported that **Burns** only decided to play after his wife gave birth to their daughter earlier than expected. The Open's official story on Burns says he had all but withdrawn before that changed the schedule.

Then he went out and detonated the back nine.

AP said Burns holed from about **40 feet off the green at 16**, made a **20-foot birdie putt at 17**, and then birdied the brutal **18th** by holing out from a greenside bunker. The Open's recap confirms he played his final three holes in birdie-birdie-birdie and joined Herbert in the **62 club** with maybe the funniest possible finishing sequence.

So yes, Burns is only three back.

But the more important point is that the weekend now includes:

- the outright leader who nearly shot **61**
- the new dad who wasn't even sure he would be there
- and a scoreboard that still looks unstable enough to do something weird

Perfect.

## DeChambeau's Penalty Changed More Than His Number

The other big Friday development was not a birdie.

It was the ruling.

The Open's official round-two report says **DeChambeau** was assessed a **two-stroke penalty under Rule 8.1** on the **5th hole**, and **Grant Moir**, chief referee for **The Open**, said the rule applies even when there is no intent to improve the area. AP reported the penalty turned DeChambeau's **66** into a **68**, dropping him from **one shot behind Herbert** to **three shots back**.

That is not minor bookkeeping.

That changed the shape of the weekend.

Instead of entering Saturday breathing directly down the leader's neck, **Bryson** moved into a tie at **5-under** with Burns and **Si Woo Kim**. It also made the whole upper board feel less settled, because now the line between "in the final group" and "just another chaser" came down to one late rules review.

Whether people enjoy that is their problem.
It still matters.

## The Weekend Chase Pack Is Better Than a Normal Major Script

One reason Friday landed so well is that the board did not become top-heavy after the record rounds.

It stayed layered.

The Open's official recap says **Suber**, **Young**, and **Gerard** are right there at **6-under**. **Tommy Fleetwood**, **Robert MacIntyre**, **Matt Wallace**, and **Alex Fitzpatrick** are another shot back. AP and the official leaderboard both show **Scottie Scheffler** at **4-under** after a second straight **68**, close enough to feel dangerous without being in cruise control. **Rory McIlroy** improved with a **67** to get to **1-under**, which is not comfortable but is at least alive.

That is the useful halfway shape:

- enough stars to keep the weekend heavy
- enough fringe names to keep it from feeling preapproved
- and enough rules weirdness to remind everyone that links majors do not have to be tidy

That is a championship worth following.

## Bottom Line

**Lucas Herbert** leads **The 154th Open** at **8-under** after a record-tying **62**, according to **AP's July 17 report** and **The Open's official round-two coverage** checked on **Saturday, July 18, 2026**.

**Sam Burns** matched that **62** to get to **5-under**, while **Bryson DeChambeau's** late **two-stroke penalty** dropped him from one back to three back and changed the entire weekend board.

That is a halfway leaderboard with actual juice, not just famous names standing in the right order.
