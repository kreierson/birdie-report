There is a certain kind of golfer who gets to **Bandon Dunes**, hears the words "template holes," "massive greens," and "Tom Doak plus Jim Urbina riffing on Charles Blair Macdonald," then immediately starts acting like they are about to have a spiritual breakthrough.

That golfer can be annoying.

The annoying part does not make them wrong.

**Old Macdonald** really does sound like one of the smartest rounds on property if you love width off the tee, weird green contours, and courses that keep asking whether you actually meant that target.

This is not a fake firsthand review where I pretend I just walked off 18 muttering about biarritz swales and ghost lines like a podcast developed a body. This is a practical review built from Bandon Dunes' current official **Old Macdonald** course page, current **2026** green-fee page, and current resort FAQ and caddie information checked on **July 11, 2026**.

The question is simple:

**Is Old Macdonald actually worth prioritizing in 2026, or is it just the Bandon round architecture people guilt you into pretending to understand?**

Yes, it looks worth it.

And no, you do not need to cosplay as a golden-age scholar to enjoy it.

## Quick Verdict

**Old Macdonald is worth it** if you want:

- the widest fairways and biggest green complexes in the Bandon main-course rotation
- a round that rewards angles, trajectory, and restraint more than fairway-finding panic
- a course that feels different from [Bandon Dunes](/blog/bandon-dunes-review-2026/), [Pacific Dunes](/blog/pacific-dunes-review-2026/), and [Sheep Ranch](/blog/sheep-ranch-review-2026/) instead of just louder or prettier
- one of the strongest strategy-first rounds on a trip that already has plenty of scenery

It is not the best fit if your whole Bandon dream is nonstop ocean-cliff theatrics or if giant greens mostly sound like a long-form three-putt prank.

## What Old Macdonald Actually Is

Bandon's current official course page describes **Old Macdonald** as the resort's fourth course and says it pays homage to **Charles Blair Macdonald** by asking what he might have created if the Oregon coast had been his canvas.

The same current page says **Tom Doak** and **Jim Urbina** built a course of:

- **vast greens**
- **myriad angles of play**
- and **fierce bunkers**

That is not subtle marketing copy.

It also happens to explain the golf pretty well.

Old Mac sounds like a course that wants you thinking about:

- which side of the fairway actually matters
- whether the aggressive line is worth the approach angle it buys
- and whether "hitting the green" means anything useful when the green is the size of a county fairground

If you want the broader resort order first, start with the main [Bandon Dunes resort guide](/blog/best-golf-courses-bandon-dunes-oregon/). If you already know you are choosing between the main-property heavyweights, this is where Old Mac starts separating itself.

## Why Old Mac Has Such a Strong Case

### It sounds wide off the tee and still mentally exhausting in a good way

That is not a contradiction.

It is the entire appeal.

A lot of golfers hear "wide fairways" and assume the course is soft.

Old Mac does not read soft. It reads like a course that gives you enough room to swing, then makes you prove you knew where the next shot needed to come from.

That is a much more interesting kind of difficulty than simple narrow-target punishment.

### The official hole notes keep pushing smart angles over dumb heroism

The current hole-by-hole notes on Bandon's official page make the point over and over:

- **No. 1** says you should check hole location for the best angle into the green and add enough club when the north wind is up.
- **No. 3** gives you a real choice: draw driver around the cedar for more upside or hit hybrid or middle iron and accept a short-iron second.
- **No. 6** tells you to avoid the cross bunker, lay up for position, and approach from the left side for the best angle.
- **No. 8** says the best shot is a lower-trajectory play that lands short and uses the swale.
- **No. 10** warns that the elevated green plays smaller than it looks and says par is a good score.

That is catnip for golfers who like strategy and a giant warning label for golfers who only want to bash driver and ask questions later.

### It may be the best "second or third round" Bandon booking

This is an inference from the current official material and the rest of the resort lineup, not a direct Bandon claim.

But Old Mac sounds especially strong once you have already felt Bandon wind and firm turf once.

I still think the original [Bandon Dunes review](/blog/bandon-dunes-review-2026/) makes the smartest first-round case for a lot of golfers, and [Bandon Trails](/blog/bandon-trails-review-2026/) is probably the cleaner variety play if you want forest and meadow texture.

Old Mac feels more like the round you book once your trip is already moving and you want the full strategy-brain version of Bandon.

## The Current 2026 Money Is the Same Big-Boy Bandon Math

Bandon Dunes' current official **2026 green-fee** page lists the same main rates for **Old Macdonald** as **Bandon Dunes**, **Pacific Dunes**, **Bandon Trails**, and **Sheep Ranch**.

That means:

- **resort guests** pay **$375 from June through September**
- **day guests** pay **$425 from June through September**
- **resort guests** pay **$340 in May**
- **day guests** pay **$390 in May**
- **replay rounds** drop to **$190 from June through September** and **$170 in May**

That is expensive.

But at least it is not fake-expensive mystery math.

The same page also says:

- all courses are **walking only**
- day guests can book **April through mid-November** more than **21 days in advance** at a **premium rate**
- and those premium day-guest times must be **after 10:00 a.m.**

So yes, this is public-access golf in theory.

In practice, Bandon still very clearly prefers the resort-stay model.

## Walking-Only Is Part of the Experience, Not a Side Note

The current official FAQ says **Bandon Dunes is walking-only**, with riding carts generally limited to golfers with a permanent disability that prevents walking.

The same FAQ says:

- caddies are encouraged
- the caddie fee is **$125 per bag, per round plus gratuity**
- and complimentary **pull/push carts** are available

On Old Mac specifically, the caddie case feels especially strong.

Not because you cannot survive without one.

Because a course built around:

- angles
- wind
- giant surfaces
- and wrong-side mistakes

...is exactly the kind of place where local guidance has real value instead of ceremonial luxury vibes.

## The Aerification Dates Matter More Here Than on Simpler Courses

Bandon's current FAQ says **any course played within five days of its aerification gets a 25% discount off the greens fee**.

For **Old Macdonald**, the current **2026** aerification windows listed are:

- **March 31 through April 2, 2026**
- **October 6 through October 8, 2026**

That is worth checking because huge greens and contour-heavy golf are not the place where I want to talk myself into "it is probably fine" if the surfaces are compromised.

If you are paying Bandon money for template-golf funhouse greens, I would rather hit the clean window.

## How I Would Actually Approach the Round

### 1. Stop treating width as permission

Old Mac sounds generous off the tee and punishing right after that if you get lazy.

Width is useful.

It is not a substitute for choosing the correct half of the fairway.

If your default move is seeing room and swinging harder, reread [how to play crosswinds without overcorrecting](/blog/how-to-play-crosswinds-without-overcorrecting/) and [how to save a round when driver is gone](/blog/how-to-save-a-round-when-driver-is-gone/) before you go.

### 2. Use the ground on purpose

The official note on **No. 8** explicitly points you toward a lower-flight shot that lands short and runs through the swale.

That is not a random detail. That is Old Mac telling you how it wants to be played.

If your whole game is still built on flying everything all the way to the number, [how to hit knockdown wedges in the wind](/blog/how-to-hit-knockdown-wedges-in-the-wind/) and [how to play golf in the wind](/blog/how-to-play-golf-in-the-wind/) are worth revisiting.

### 3. Lag putting is not optional here

Massive greens are fun until they turn into 70-foot judgment tests.

If you are heading to Old Mac without decent pace control, you are basically planning a stress event. Go read [lag putting tips to eliminate three-putts](/blog/lag-putting-tips-eliminate-three-putts/) first and stop pretending distance control on giant greens is a side skill.

## Who Should Play It

### Play it if you like architecture without needing to announce it

Old Mac has a very real case for golfers who enjoy strategy, options, and holes that keep revealing themselves as you play them.

### Play it if you want the most brainy Bandon round

Not the prettiest.
Not the easiest first date.
Probably not the simplest.

But maybe the most likely to stay stuck in your head afterward for the actual golf.

### Pass it if your Bandon trip is all coastline and no curiosity

That is a valid preference.

It is just a preference, not a quality argument.

## Bottom Line

**Old Macdonald looks absolutely worth prioritizing in 2026** if you want a Bandon round built around width, angles, giant greens, and strategy that never really leaves you alone.

The current official facts are pretty clear:

- same premium main-course pricing as the other Bandon heavyweights
- walking-only setup
- **$125 per bag plus gratuity** caddie math
- and aerification windows on **March 31-April 2, 2026** and **October 6-8, 2026**

That does not make Old Mac cheap.

It does make the case cleaner.

If your favorite golf feels a little weird, a little strategic, and a little rude in the best possible way, **Old Macdonald might be the Bandon round you end up loving most**.
