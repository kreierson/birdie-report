Buy the **Callaway Opus** if you want the smarter recommendation for most golfers.

Buy the **Titleist Vokey SM10** if you already know you like the Vokey shape, care about grind fit, and want the premium older-generation bargain instead of the simpler Callaway buy.

My honest split:

- **Opus** for the golfer who wants the practical value lane
- **SM10** for the golfer who treats wedges like scoring tools first and status symbols second

## Why the Opus Gets the Default Recommendation

The **Callaway Opus** is one of the easiest current players-wedge recommendations because it lands in the sweet spot between:

- price sanity
- real spin tech
- useful loft and grind coverage
- a traditional shape that still feels like a real wedge

That combination matters a lot.

The Opus does not try to win by being the most obsessive wedge on the market.

It wins by being easier to buy correctly.

That is a real advantage for a huge percentage of golfers, especially the ones shopping wedges without a fitter standing over their shoulder correcting their life choices.

## Why the SM10 Still Has a Strong Case

The **Vokey SM10** stops looking like "last year's premium wedge" the moment the number drops enough to matter.

And in the current site coverage, it does matter.

At around **$159**, the SM10 becomes a very annoying wedge to dismiss because you are still getting:

- the classic Vokey visual
- a bigger configuration story
- strong half-shot feel credibility
- the comfort of a wedge ecosystem golfers already understand

That is the real appeal.

Not "buy this because Vokey is Vokey."

Buy this because the price no longer demands that it be the unquestioned king to make sense.

If the newer-generation question is the one actually bothering you, read [Titleist Vokey SM11 vs Titleist Vokey SM10](/blog/titleist-vokey-sm11-vs-titleist-vokey-sm10/) next.

## Price: Opus Still Wins the Easier Math

If you are replacing two or three wedges, every ten or twenty bucks matters more than golfers like to admit.

The **Opus** still has the cleaner up-front value case.

It gives you a modern wedge with enough seriousness to satisfy an improving golfer without drifting into "I guess I just spent driver money on my wedge setup" territory.

That said, the **SM10** becomes dangerous once the price gap compresses.

At that point, the question changes from:

"Why would I pay for the older Vokey?"

to:

"Why would I not, if I already prefer the Vokey look and fit logic?"

So yes, **Opus** wins the simpler math.

But **SM10** wins the more tempting premium discount argument.

## Grind and Fit: Vokey Still Gives You More Ways to Care

This is the category where Vokey buyers feel smug, and sometimes they have a point.

The **SM10** gives golfers a deeper menu of loft, bounce, and grind combinations than the **Opus**.

That matters if:

- you already know what your turf interaction needs
- you open the face a lot
- you are picky about full-swing versus finesse-wedge behavior
- you are actually getting fit instead of improv-shopping

The **Opus** lineup is smarter for everyone else because its **S, W, C, and T** grind story is broad enough to be useful without turning into a homework assignment.

So the real answer is:

- **SM10** if you want more specific tools
- **Opus** if you want a better chance of buying the right wedge on the first try

## Feel and Greenside Confidence

This category is less objective, but it still matters because wedges are scoring clubs, not appliances.

The **SM10** still has the stronger reputation for:

- partial-shot feel
- feedback
- that classic Vokey sense that the head is doing exactly what you asked

The **Opus** fights back by being more than good enough while costing less and asking less of your wallet and attention span.

So if you are the golfer who really notices wedge feel and uses a bunch of different scoring shots, the **SM10** earns its case.

If you mostly want reliable spin, sane grinds, and a cleaner value story, the **Opus** makes more sense.
