The **8-15 yard** pitch is where golfers start lying to themselves.

They call it a chip when it is not really a chip.
They call it a pitch when they do not actually have a pitch they trust.
Then they make some weird half-stab with a lob wedge and act offended when the ball comes out dead, thin, or ten feet hotter than planned.

This shot is not hard because it is magical.

It is hard because it sits right in the gap between two ideas:

- too much carry for a simple runner
- not enough room for a full-feel stock pitch

If you can putt it or bump it, do that first. The basic [chip-shot route](/blog/chip-shot-technique-around-the-green/) is still the easiest adult answer whenever the lie and green let you get away with it. If the shot stretches past this awkward band, move into the cleaner [15-30 yard pitch system](/blog/how-to-control-15-to-30-yard-pitch-shots/) and the broader [30-80 yard scoring-zone guide](/blog/pitch-shot-distance-control-scoring-zone/).

This piece is for the annoying little middle:

- a bit too far for your safe chip
- a bit too short for a comfortable pitch
- exactly long enough to expose every decel problem you own

## The Job From 8-15 Yards

You are not trying to hit a one-hop TV spinner.

You are trying to:

- carry the first problem cleanly
- land the ball in a predictable first bounce window
- finish inside about **6 feet** when you hit it well
- keep the bad miss on the green or fringe

That is the whole assignment.

From this distance, a shot inside **4 feet** is awesome.
Inside **6 feet** is very useful.
Inside **10 feet** is still not a crime.

What kills scores is not missing by six feet.
It is chunking one three yards, blading the next one twelve paces long, then pretending the issue was "touch."

No. The issue was that the shot had no structure.

## Use the 2-Bounce Ladder

This is the easiest way I know to simplify this yardage.

Instead of thinking in vague soft-hands nonsense, choose your first two bounces.

| Shot | Carry Window | First Bounce | Second Bounce | Rollout | Best Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **One-bounce pop** | **8-11 yards** | just on the green | quick second bounce, softer release | **2-5 feet** | front pins, tiny landing shelves, short-sided lies with enough loft |
| **Two-bounce checker** | **11-15 yards** | **1-3 feet** on | second bounce around **4-6 feet** on | **4-8 feet** | normal mini-pitches, middle pins, safer default option |

The **two-bounce checker** should be your default.

It uses a slightly longer motion, a lower stress level, and a much smaller disaster pattern.

The **one-bounce pop** exists because sometimes the pin is tucked in a place that punishes rollout immediately.

What I do not want is golfers choosing the pop shot every time because it looks more talented.

That is how you create the classic mini-pitch double:

- one chunk
- one blade
- one muttered cuss word

## My Stock Club Rule

For most golfers, this shot does not need three different wedges.

I like this simple starting map:

- **54° or 56°** for the default two-bounce checker
- **58° or 60°** for the one-bounce pop when the landing shelf is genuinely tiny

If you are between clubs, choose the option that lets you make the **slightly longer, calmer motion** unless the pin absolutely forces more height.

That keeps the strike cleaner and the rollout more honest.

## The Setup Numbers That Matter

This shot gets easier fast when the setup is boring.

My stock checklist:

- stance slightly open
- feet about **one clubhead wider than your shoulders are narrow**, not full-swing wide
- pressure **60 to 65 percent** on the lead foot
- ball **center to one ball back of center**
- grip down about **1 inch**
- hands only **slightly** ahead, not rammed halfway to the target

That is enough.

Do not add some giant forward press.
Do not hang back trying to help it up.
Do not widen the stance because the shot makes you nervous.

If your contact is still ugly from this setup, revisit [how to stop chunking chip shots](/blog/how-to-stop-chunking-chip-shots/) and [how to chip tight lies without blading it](/blog/how-to-chip-tight-lies-without-blading-it/). The same low-point problem usually keeps showing up in new clothes.

## What the Motion Should Feel Like

My favorite feel on this shot is:

**brush the grass to the landing window.**

That keeps your brain on the right job.

For both stock patterns, I want:

- chest and arms moving together
- a shorter backswing than you think
- a committed through-swing
- no panic wrist throw trying to manufacture loft

The difference between the two shots is small:

- **one-bounce pop:** a little more loft, a little more hinge, slightly shorter landing spot
- **two-bounce checker:** a little lower, a little more through, slightly longer landing spot

The fatal move is always the same:

- take it back too far
- slow down through the ball
- try to guide the face under it

That is the decel cocktail that creates both chunks and skulls.

## The Three Questions That Clean Up the Decision

### 1. What has to carry?

If the fringe, slope, or collar starts at **7 yards**, then your carry number matters more than the pin at **12**.

Start with the carry.
Always.

### 2. Is this actually a mini-pitch or just a chip with ambition?

If a pitching wedge or gap wedge can land it safely on and let it release, do not overcomplicate the shot because loft feels cooler.

### 3. Which option has the smaller ugly miss?

For most amateurs, the answer is the two-bounce checker.

If the pop shot sometimes flies **6 yards** and sometimes **16**, it is not your stock answer. It is your emergency answer.

## The Most Common Mistakes

### 1. Trying to help it up

The loft already handles that.

Your job is clean contact and correct landing spot.

### 2. Choosing the highest shot because the pin is visible

Seeing a front pin does not automatically mean you earned the hero version.

### 3. Practicing one perfect lie and calling it short-game work

That teaches nothing useful.

This distance gets ugly from:

- slight rough
- grainy collar
- downhill stance
- tight bare-ish lie

That is where the real scoring work lives.

## Drill 1: The 3-Towel Bounce Ladder

Put three towels or targets on the green:

- **1 foot on**
- **3 feet on**
- **6 feet on**

From **8-15 yards**, hit:

- **4 one-bounce pop shots** trying to land near the **1-foot** towel
- **4 two-bounce checkers** trying to land near the **3-foot** towel
- **4 more two-bounce checkers** trying to land near the **6-foot** second-bounce window

Rules:

- any shot that comes up short of the green is an automatic zero
- any shot that races more than **10 feet** past the hole is an automatic zero

Goal:

- **8 of 12** solid reps means you have a real system
- **10 of 12** means this shot is becoming a weapon

## Drill 2: The 9-Ball Landing Test

Drop nine balls from mixed lies inside **15 yards**.

Before every shot, say out loud:

- club
- shot type
- landing spot

Scoring:

- **2 points:** finishes inside **6 feet**
- **1 point:** finishes on the green inside **10 feet**
- **0 points:** short, chunked, bladed, or obviously wrong plan

Good score:

- **11+** means the shot is playable
- **14+** means you should trust it on the course

## Drill 3: One Club, Six Lies

Take one wedge only, preferably your **56°**.

Hit six balls from:

- clean fairway
- light rough
- collar
- slight downhill lie
- slight uphill lie
- short-sided lie

The point is not variety for variety's sake.

The point is learning whether your stock motion actually survives contact changes.

If it does not, you do not own the shot yet.

## My Default On-Course Rules

These rules save more strokes than creativity does.

### Rule 1: Use the two-bounce checker first

Only switch to the one-bounce pop when the green setup actually demands it.

### Rule 2: Name the landing spot before you pull the trigger

If you cannot point to the first bounce, you are still guessing.

### Rule 3: Keep the bad miss long, not dead

A putt from **8 feet** is annoying.
A chunk that never gets onto the green is much dumber.

## Bottom Line

The **8-15 yard pitch** gets easier the second you stop treating it like a mystery touch shot.

Use:

- one stock **two-bounce checker**
- one emergency **one-bounce pop**
- simple setup numbers
- and drills that force you to pick a landing plan instead of freelancing

That is enough to turn one of the stupidest little scoring-zone yardages into something useful.

Not sexy.
Just useful.

That is better.
