Short-sided bunker shots make otherwise normal golfers start believing in stupid things.

They see five paces of green, a lip that looks taller than it is, and a pin tucked like the superintendent took something personally.

Then they try one of two terrible plans:

- they baby the swing and leave it in the bunker
- or they panic-hit a full violence splash and blade it twenty yards over the green

Neither one is adult golf.

If your bunker issue is just getting the ball out at all, start with [bunker shot basics](/blog/bunker-shot-basics-get-out-every-time/). If the ball is half-buried, go straight to [how to hit buried bunker lies](/blog/how-to-hit-buried-bunker-lies/). If the shot is longer and you have room to use rollout, read [long greenside bunker shots from 20 to 40 yards](/blog/long-greenside-bunker-shots-20-to-40-yards/).

This piece is for the annoying middle ground:

- standard lie
- little green
- real lip
- and a shot that punishes half-commitment immediately

## The First Truth: You Are Not Trying to Hit a Tour-Spinner

You are trying to leave yourself a putt you can live with.

That is the whole shift.

From a short-sided bunker shot, I want most decent amateurs thinking:

- **inside 12 feet** = good result
- **inside 6 feet** = hell yes
- **on the green with an uphill putt** = still fine

The scorecard damage usually comes from demanding a two-foot leave on a shot that never deserved that expectation.

This is the same broader scoring logic from [save more pars after missed greens](/blog/save-more-pars-after-missed-greens/): stop asking the short game to clean up decision-making you should have done earlier.

## The 3-Window System

This is the simplest way I know to stop bunker indecision.

Instead of standing there thinking about "touch" and "feel" and other vague nonsense, pick one of three landing windows.

### Window 1: Just-On Window

Use this when:

- the pin is **2 to 5 paces** onto the green
- the green slopes away from you
- or the bunker lip makes you throw it high quickly

Your landing spot:

- **1 to 3 feet** onto the putting surface

This is the highest, softest version.

### Window 2: Safe-Spin Window

Use this when:

- the pin is short-sided but not absurd
- you have **5 to 8 paces** of green
- and the surface is not racing away

Your landing spot:

- about **4 to 7 feet** onto the green

This is the default window for most playable short-sided bunker shots.

### Window 3: Take-Your-Medicine Window

Use this when:

- the lip is tall
- the sand is weird
- the ball is sitting a little down
- or the pin location is flirting with malpractice

Your landing spot:

- **middle section of the green**, even if that means **12 to 20 feet** past the hole

This is not weak.

This is the shot that keeps doubles off the card when the sexy option was fake from the start.

If you need help recognizing those pin situations before you short-side yourself in the first place, read [how to play front pins without making bogey](/blog/how-to-play-front-pins-without-making-bogey/) and [stop short-siding yourself](/blog/stop-short-siding-yourself-approach-strategy/).

## The Setup Numbers That Matter

Short-sided bunker shots are not magic. They are setup and commitment.

Here is my stock checklist.

### 1. Open the face first, then grip it

Same rule as every basic greenside bunker shot.

I want the face:

- **noticeably open** for Window 1
- **slightly open** for Window 2
- **moderately open or even just a touch open** for Window 3 if the lie or lip is sketchy

Do not grip first and then twist the face as an afterthought. That usually turns into panic hands through impact.

### 2. Put the ball forward, but not off the planet

Use:

- front heel to just inside front heel for Window 1
- front heel to one ball back for Window 2
- one ball back of normal bunker position for Window 3

The more aggressive the height requirement, the more forward the ball can live.

### 3. Favor the lead side and keep it there

I want:

- **60 to 65 percent** pressure on the lead foot

Then keep it there.

No hanging back.
No scooping.
No trying to help the ball up.

The sand already knows how to do that job.

### 4. Pick the entry point before you swing

This is the number golfers always skip.

My stock entry points:

- **2 to 2.5 inches** behind the ball for Window 1
- **1.5 to 2 inches** behind the ball for Window 2
- **1 to 1.5 inches** behind the ball for Window 3

That is the whole trade:

more sand for height and softness, less sand for forward energy and damage control.

## What the Swing Should Feel Like

My favorite bunker thought for these shots is:

**splash the landing window, not the ball.**

That keeps the brain on the right problem.

For Window 1 especially, the swing needs to feel bigger than the distance suggests.

That is where golfers screw it up.

The pin is close, so they make a scared little motion and hope loft saves them.

It will not.

The open face and sand are already taking speed away. You need enough swing to move through the sand with purpose.

My rough feels:

- **Window 1:** full chest turn, committed finish, plenty of splash
- **Window 2:** same commitment, slightly less face, slightly thinner sand
- **Window 3:** more forward, more through, less cute

What I do **not** want:

- deceleration
- stop-and-lift arms
- or some hand-flippy little rescue move because the lip looks mean

That is how you blade one into orbit.

## The Two Questions That Pick the Window Fast

When you get to the bunker, ask:

### 1. How much green do I honestly have?

Not emotionally.
Not optimistically.
Actually.

Count paces if you need to.

- **2 to 5 paces** usually pushes you toward Window 1
- **5 to 8 paces** usually fits Window 2
- weird lie, nasty slope, or fake pin location pushes you toward Window 3

### 2. What is the worst miss?

That is the money question.

If the worst miss from Window 1 is:

- still short in the bunker
- or bladed over the green

then maybe Window 1 was not available to you in the first place.

If Window 3 gives you:

- middle green
- twenty feet
- and a boring two-putt chance

that may be the smart shot even if the hole location is begging you to act dumber.

## The Club Choice Rule

Use the most loft that clears the lip without forcing you into nonsense.

Usually that means:

- **lob wedge** for the highest Window 1 versions
- **sand wedge** for most Window 2 shots
- **sand wedge or gap wedge** for Window 3 only if the lip is modest and the ball is sitting clean

Most amateurs do not need more tricks.

They need one predictable bunker club and a cleaner window choice.

## Three Mistakes That Kill Short-Sided Sand Shots

### 1. Trying to be delicate

This is the big one.

You do not need a softer swing.
You need the correct amount of sand with a committed motion.

### 2. Picking Window 1 when Window 3 was the adult answer

Some pins are bait.

Do not eat all of it.

### 3. Looking at the hole too early

Your brain sees the tiny amount of green and starts steering the clubhead at the ball.

That usually changes the bottom of the arc and exposes the leading edge.

Pick the landing window.
Trust the entry point.
Then rip the bandaid off.

## Drill 1: The Three-Towel Landing Drill

Put three towels or targets on the green:

- one **2 feet** onto the surface
- one **5 feet** on
- one in the **middle section**

Hit **9 balls**:

- 3 to Window 1
- 3 to Window 2
- 3 to Window 3

Scoring:

- **2 points** if the ball lands in the right window and finishes on the green
- **1 point** if it lands close to the window but misses the target section
- **0 points** if it stays in the bunker or goes flying long

Benchmarks:

- **12 points**: usable
- **15 points**: solid
- **17 or better**: you are actually practicing instead of just surviving

## Drill 2: The One-Line Entry Drill

Draw a line in the sand.

Then set six balls with the back of each ball just ahead of that line.

Hit:

- 2 shots entering **2.5 inches** behind
- 2 shots entering **2 inches** behind
- 2 shots entering **1.5 inches** behind

This teaches you that not every bunker shot needs the same chunk of sand.

That is the whole point of the windows.

## Drill 3: The Up-and-Down Test

Drop **6 balls** in short-sided bunker spots around the practice green.

Play every ball all the way out.

Track:

- how many get on the green
- how many finish inside **12 feet**
- how many become actual up-and-downs

Targets:

- **6 of 6** on the green
- **4 of 6** inside 12 feet
- **2 of 6** up-and-downs

If you cannot clear those marks in practice, stop trying to hit highlight-reel sand shots on the course.

## My On-Course Rule

From a short-sided greenside bunker shot, I want one thought:

**choose the window that removes the disaster first.**

That usually means:

- Window 1 when the lie is clean and the lip forces height
- Window 2 when you have a little usable green
- Window 3 when the hole is trying to bait you into making six

That is it.

Not every short-sided bunker shot needs bravery.
Some of them just need honesty.

## Bottom Line

Short-sided bunker shots get a lot easier when you stop treating them like one mystery shot.

Use the **3-window system**:

- **Window 1:** 1 to 3 feet on
- **Window 2:** 4 to 7 feet on
- **Window 3:** middle green and no apology

Match the entry point to the window.
Keep the face open enough for the job.
Keep the weight forward.
And make a committed swing instead of a scared little negotiation.

Do that, and these shots stop feeling like emergency golf and start feeling like the kind of boring, competent sand saves that actually lower scores.
