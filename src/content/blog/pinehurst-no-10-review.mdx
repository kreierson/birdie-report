There is a point on a lot of Pinehurst trips where the conversation gets a little too tidy.

No. 2 is the history round.  
No. 4 is the smarter fun round.  
The Cradle is the short-course beer round.

And then **Pinehurst No. 10** shows up and reminds everyone the resort did not build a new course just to hand you another polished clubhouse-core remix.

This is not a fake firsthand review where I pretend I just hoofed 18 through the Sandmines and developed a spiritual bond with every sandy shoulder. This is a practical review built from Pinehurst's current official **No. 10** course page, current FAQ and package rules, and the resort's current **2026** maintenance schedule.

The question is simple:

**Is Pinehurst No. 10 actually worth prioritizing in 2026 if you want the modern, walking-only Pinehurst round that feels most different from the old core?**

Yeah.

It has a very real case.

## Quick Verdict

**Pinehurst No. 10 is worth it** if you want:

- the most rugged and modern-feeling premium round in the Pinehurst orbit
- a walking-only day that feels less manicured and more exploratory
- a Tom Doak design that gives Pinehurst a different visual and strategic texture
- a second or third resort headline round that is not just "more Pinehurst" in the familiar sense

It is not the move if you only care about Ross history, need the easiest value case, or want a Pinehurst trip that stays close to the main-resort comfort bubble the whole time.

If you want the big-picture Pinehurst map first, read [best golf courses in Pinehurst, North Carolina](/blog/best-golf-courses-pinehurst-north-carolina/), [Pinehurst No. 2 review](/blog/pinehurst-no-2-review/), and [Pinehurst No. 4 review](/blog/pinehurst-no-4-review/). This page is for the golfer who already knows the trip is happening and wants to know whether No. 10 is the Sandmines detour worth paying attention to.

## What Pinehurst No. 10 Actually Is

Pinehurst's current official **No. 10** page says the course is the resort's **first new course in nearly three decades** and that the landscape features **native wiregrass, sweeping landscapes, rolling hills, and rugged dunes carved out by mining operations around the turn of the 20th century**.

That is the entire pitch right there.

This is Pinehurst leaning into land that sounds bigger, rougher, and less country-club tidy than the resort's central legend.

The same course page says No. 10 shares its home with **Pinehurst Sandmines**, a mostly undeveloped **900-acre** property, and lists the essentials:

- **Tom Doak design**
- **7,020 yards**
- **Par 70**
- **Opening year 2024**

That is useful because it tells you No. 10 is not trying to cosplay as Ross.

It is trying to be its own thing.

## Why No. 10 Has Such a Strong Case

### It gives Pinehurst a modern adventure round instead of another heritage lecture

There is nothing wrong with heritage. Pinehurst obviously does that better than almost anyone.

But if every premium course on the property asked the same questions with slightly different fonts, the whole destination would start feeling repetitive.

No. 10 sounds like the antidote.

The Sandmines setting, the mining-cut dunes, and the Tom Doak routing language all point toward a round that should feel:

- more expansive
- more rugged
- more walking-dependent
- and a little less precious

That is good.

Pinehurst did not need another course that just repeated what No. 2 and No. 4 already cover.

### It looks like the cleanest "third lane" after No. 2 and No. 4

The existing Pinehurst hierarchy already makes sense:

- **No. 2** is the history-and-turtleback exam
- **No. 4** is the broader-appeal premium splurge

No. 10 looks like the sharper modern branch.

That is what makes it compelling.

If No. 2 explains why Pinehurst matters and No. 4 explains why the place can still be enjoyable for normal humans, No. 10 looks like the course that proves the resort still wants to experiment instead of just monetizing nostalgia.

### Walking-only changes the feel in a good way

Pinehurst's current FAQ says the resort highly encourages caddies on **No. 10** because it is **walking only**, and separately states that **No. 10 is a walking only course** while carts are allowed on most others.

That matters.

Walking-only is not just a logistics note. It shapes the day.

It tells you this round is supposed to be absorbed properly, not skimmed from a cart while you check a screen and pretend the land is a side plot.

## The Access Rules Are Still Real Resort Rules

Pinehurst's current FAQ says that to play **courses 2, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, or 10**, you must be a **member or resort guest**.

So no, this is not the sneaky public back door into Pinehurst relevance.

It is still a proper resort-planning round.

That makes the package structure matter more than a single posted green fee. Pinehurst's current **Premier Golf Package** says it includes **all advance and replay rounds**, and that the package **bundles all golf fees and course premiums**, explicitly giving guests the option to **discover the new No. 10** alongside No. 2, The Cradle, and the classic courses.

That is useful because it clarifies the fit:

- No. 10 is not really a one-off casual add
- it makes the most sense as part of a broader Pinehurst stay
- and it gets more attractive when it is one branch inside a bigger golf package rather than a standalone "is this alone worth it?" argument

## The Caddie and Walking Math Matter

Pinehurst's current FAQ says:

- **Caddie: $85 per bag**
- **Forecaddie: $45 per player**
- **Single bag: $100**

It also says Pinehurst recommends a single-strap carry bag under **24 pounds** for caddie use.

That is not trivial money.

On No. 10, I still think it makes sense.

This sounds like the kind of round where:

- local lines matter
- the scale of the property matters
- and conserving energy on a walking-only day matters

My take:

- **take a caddie** if this is a one-shot bucket-list Pinehurst build
- **take a forecaddie** if your group wants help but does not want to go full spend
- **carry or push** only if you already know walking golf is actually your lane and not something you claim to enjoy once a year

## Timing the Trip Still Matters

Pinehurst's current **2026 Course Maintenance Schedule** lists **No. 10** for maintenance on **July 20-30, 2026** and lists **Monday afternoon** as the course's regular topdressing slot from **May 1 through August 31**.

That is exactly the kind of detail you want before spending Pinehurst money.

If you are planning a summer trip, ignore that and you are just volunteering to get annoyed by something the resort already told you in advance.

## How I Would Actually Approach No. 10

### 1. Treat it like a walking round first and a scorecard second

That does not mean be casual.

It means pace yourself like an adult.

Eat early. Hydrate. Do not show up under-loose and then pretend the walk surprised you.

That feels like a complete trip.
