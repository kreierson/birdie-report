Here's the thing about putting — it's roughly 40% of your total strokes and the one area where equipment can genuinely compensate for bad technique. You don't need a $400 Scotty Cameron. You need forgiveness, alignment, and a putter that rolls the ball where you're looking.

If you're a high handicapper averaging 36+ putts per round, the right putter won't fix your green reading. But it *will* turn those 5-foot knee-knockers from coin flips into makeable putts. And it'll keep your lag putts inside 3 feet instead of leaving you 6-footers for your second putt.

Here's what actually matters when you're shopping.

## What High Handicappers Need in a Putter

**Forgiveness first.** When you miss the sweet spot (and you will), a high-MOI mallet design keeps the face from twisting. That 3-footer you pulled slightly? With a forgiving mallet, it still goes in. With a blade? It lips out and you throw your putter in the lake.

**Alignment aids.** If you can't aim the thing, nothing else matters. Lines, dots, contrasting colors — whatever helps you consistently set up square to your target. The [2-Ball alignment system](/blog/scotty-cameron-vs-odyssey-putters) from Odyssey literally changed the game for this reason.

**Face insert.** A soft face insert promotes a consistent roll regardless of where you hit it on the face. Milled faces feel great for low handicappers with precise strokes. For everyone else, an insert is more forgiving.

**Weight.** Heavier putters (350g+) are generally more stable through the stroke. If you have the yips or a wristy stroke, a heavier putter calms things down.

## Mallet vs. Blade: Which Shape Do You Need?

For high handicappers, **mallet. Almost always mallet.**

Here's the simple version: mallets have more weight distributed around the perimeter, which means they resist twisting on off-center hits. Blades are smaller, lighter, and require more precise contact.

The exception? If you have a strong arc in your stroke (the putter opens and closes significantly), a blade with toe hang might actually suit you better. But if you're not sure, go mallet. You can always switch later.
