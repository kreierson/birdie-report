Uneven lies do not need tour-level genius.

They need two things most golfers skip:

- an honest read of what the slope is going to do
- and a setup that matches the slope instead of fighting it

The big mistake is simple.

You get a weird lie, keep your normal flat-ground setup, make your normal swing, then act surprised when the ball starts left, leaks right, comes out thin, or digs halfway to China.

That is not bad luck.

That is a bad setup.

If you want to score better from slopes, stop trying to memorize fifty tiny swing thoughts. Use one clean system that works across the four lies that matter most:

- ball above your feet
- ball below your feet
- uphill lie
- downhill lie

## The 4 Checks I Want Before Every Uneven-Lie Shot

Before you pick the exact club, run these four checks:

1. **What is the slope actually doing?**
2. **What start line should I choose if the lie adds curve?**
3. **Where does the low point need to be?**
4. **Is this a scoring shot or a survival shot?**

That last one matters.

Not every uneven lie is an "attack the pin" lie. Some are just "advance the ball, miss in the cheap place, and stop the round from getting stupid" lies. If you are already in damage-control territory, read [recovery-shot strategy](/blog/recovery-shot-strategy-stop-doubles/) and stop pretending every awkward slope still owes you a birdie chance.

## The One Rule That Helps on Every Slope

Match your body to the ground.

That means:

- let your shoulders roughly follow the slope
- let your balance stay in the middle of your feet
- and make the swing feel quieter, not more violent

Uneven lies punish extra motion.

If your setup fits the hill, you usually do **not** need some heroic manipulation on the way down. You need a smaller, more balanced version of your normal swing.

## 1. Ball Above Your Feet: Expect Left and Plan for It

This is the lie that tricks golfers into fast hands and surprise hooks.

When the ball sits above your feet:

- the club effectively plays more upright
- the face wants to turn over more easily
- and the ball is more likely to start a little left and keep drawing

### My default setup

- stand a touch taller
- grip down about **1 inch**
- feel your weight in the middle of your feet, not shoved toward your toes
- make a three-quarter tempo swing, not a rescue swing

### My default aim adjustment

As a starting point:

- aim **5-10 yards right** of the final target with a short or mid-iron
- aim even farther right if the slope is severe and your stock miss already turns over

You are not trying to hit a hold-off fade from a lie begging to draw.

You are trying to pick a smarter start line and let the lie do what it was going to do anyway.

### What usually goes wrong

Golfers either:

- stand too close and hit behind it
- or panic about the left miss and steer the face open

Both are bad.

Grip down, stand tall enough to stay in posture, and make the same balanced finish you would want on flat turf.

If the target is tucked left or the miss left is expensive, this is not the moment to get cute. Use the same grown-up logic from [how to play front pins without making bogey](/blog/how-to-play-front-pins-without-making-bogey/) and [how to play back pins better](/blog/how-to-play-back-pins-better/): hit the fat side and move on.

## 2. Ball Below Your Feet: Build More Reach and Expect Right

Ball below your feet is where golfers start falling toward the ball and losing contact quality fast.

This lie tends to:

- flatten the swing plane
- make centered contact harder
- and encourage a shot that starts or leaks more to the right

### My default setup

- add knee flex
- widen your stance a little
- let the handle sit slightly closer to your thighs
- feel like your chest stays down through impact

The goal is simple: create enough room to reach the ball **without** lunging for it.

### My default aim adjustment

Start with:

- **5-10 yards left** of the final target for short and mid-irons

If the slope is steep and you already hate your contact from this lie, plan even more conservatively and favor the safer side of the green or fairway.

### Club choice checkpoint

Because contact can come out a little weaker here, I want one simple question:

**Can I still cover the front with my normal strike from this lie?**

If the answer is shaky, take the extra club.

That is especially important when the shot also brings [elevated greens](/blog/how-to-play-elevated-greens-without-coming-up-short/) or wind into the equation. Ball below your feet plus front trouble is not the time to prove your courage.

## 3. Uphill Lies: Match the Hill and Take the Extra Club

Uphill lies are usually the friendliest of the four, but golfers still screw them up by leaning back and trying to help the ball up.

You do not need to help it up.

The hill already did that.

An uphill lie tends to:

- add launch
- add spin
- reduce rollout
- and often encourage a slight draw shape

### My default setup

- match your shoulders to the slope
- keep the ball around **one ball forward** of center from your stock position
- feel pressure stay under your trail foot long enough to stay balanced

### My default club adjustment

On a full swing, start with:

- **one extra club**

If the slope is severe or the wind is into you, it can be more.

That does **not** mean swing harder.

It means take more club, make the same tempo swing, and accept the shot may launch higher and land softer.

### The main mistake

The bad version is:

- ball too far forward
- shoulders not matching the hill
- body hanging back
- flip through impact

That is how a perfectly playable lie turns into a high weak floaty thing that comes up short.

If the number is already in the danger zone, use the safer landing logic from [150-175 yard approach strategy](/blog/approach-strategy-150-175-yards/) or [175-200 yard approach strategy](/blog/approach-strategy-175-200-yards/). Not every uphill lie needs aggression. If uphill shots keep coming out high and stupidly short, the dedicated follow-up is [how to play uphill lies without leaving it short](/blog/how-to-play-uphill-lies-without-leaving-it-short/).

## 4. Downhill Lies: Keep the Chest Moving and Expect Less Height

Downhill lies are the ones that make golfers look immediately less talented.

That is because the slope wants:

- a lower launch
- a more left-to-right shape for many players
- and cleaner low-point control than most amateurs normally bring

### My default setup

- match your shoulders to the slope
- put the ball **1-2 balls back** of your normal position
- keep roughly **60-70%** of your pressure on your lead side
- feel your chest staying over the shot through impact

On shorter downhill shots, especially little pitches, that lead-side pressure can climb even more. Golf Monthly coach Anders Mankert demonstrated roughly **80%** lead-side pressure on downhill scoring-zone shots as a way to keep the low point ahead of the ball.

### My default club adjustment

If the shot still has to carry something meaningful, I usually want:

- **one extra club**

Why?

Because the lower launch can make golfers come up short even when the strike feels decent.

But the landing is also likely to release more, so this is not a flag-hunting shot. It is a landing-spot shot.

### The miss I hate most

The classic ugly miss is fat-short because the golfer never got their chest moving down the slope.

The slope is going down.
Your body has to go with it.

If you stay stuck on the back foot, the club bottoms out early and the hole gets dumb in a hurry.

## My Simple Uneven-Lie Chart

Here is the clean version:

| Lie | Start line | Ball position | Default club thought |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Ball above feet** | **5-10 yards right** | normal to slightly back | same club, quieter swing |
| **Ball below feet** | **5-10 yards left** | normal | extra club if front cover is close |
| **Uphill** | allow for slight draw | **1 ball forward** | **+1 club** |
| **Downhill** | allow for fade/right miss | **1-2 balls back** | **+1 club** if carry matters |

These are starting rules.

They are not holy scripture.

Wind, lie severity, turf, and your stock shot still matter. But if you use this chart instead of guessing, your bad misses should get a lot less dramatic.

## When to Stop Being Aggressive

There are four red-light combinations I want you spotting fast:

### 1. Ball below your feet plus front pin

That is a contact-risk lie into a short-sided target.

Center green. No debate.

### 2. Downhill lie plus forced carry

If the ball has to launch cleanly to cover water, bunker, or a false front, stop flirting with the perfect number and take the safer club.

### 3. Ball above your feet with trouble left

If the lie wants to turn it over and the course punishes left, the adult play is often aiming farther right or taking less aggressive line entirely.

### 4. Any extreme slope from long range

If you are holding hybrid or fairway wood on a big slope, remember the point is not looking brave. The point is getting the ball somewhere useful. That is exactly where [how to play flyer lies without air-mailing greens](/blog/how-to-play-flyer-lies-without-airmailing-greens/) and [save more pars after missed greens](/blog/save-more-pars-after-missed-greens/) start mattering.

## The 8-Ball Uneven-Lie Practice Drill

This is the best no-nonsense drill I know because it forces you to learn all four lies in one session.

Find four practice stations:

- ball above feet
- ball below feet
- uphill
- downhill

Hit **2 balls** from each for **8 total shots**.

Score it like this:

- **2 points** for a solid strike that starts on the planned line
- **1 point** for a playable miss
- **0 points** for a fat, thin, or obvious panic swing

Good score:

- **10 or better out of 16**

The point is not perfect shotmaking.

The point is learning whether your setup changes are actually producing the ball flight you expected.

## The Shoulder-Match Rehearsal Drill

Before you hit a single ball, rehearse this:

1. Put a club across your shoulders.
2. Set up on the slope.
3. Match the club to the ground angle.
4. Make three slow practice turns without losing balance.

Do that on uphill and downhill lies before you hit the real shot.

It is a stupidly simple way to stop setting up like the ground is flat when it very obviously is not.

## The 15-Second Uneven-Lie Routine on the Course

Use this:

1. Name the lie out loud in your head.
2. Pick the start line the lie needs.
3. Adjust ball position and posture.
4. Take the club that covers the expensive miss.
5. Make the balanced swing, not the heroic swing.

That is it.

No dissertations.
No late panic.
No pretending the slope is optional.

## Bottom Line

If you want to hit better shots from uneven lies, stop chasing miracle hand action and start fixing the setup.

The useful adjustments are boring:

- match the slope
- move the ball when the lie demands it
- aim for the curve the hill is likely to create
- and take enough club to clear the expensive mistake

Do that, and uneven lies stop feeling random.

They start feeling manageable.
