You already know which one is newer.

The real question is:

**Do you buy the older premium wedge that has finally dropped into sane money, or do you buy the current Cleveland wedge that is still reasonably priced and much easier to trust as a clean live product?**

This comparison is **research-based** and built from Birdie Report's current [Callaway Jaws Raw wedge review](/blog/callaway-jaws-raw-wedge-review/) and adjacent wedge inventory work published on **July 15, 2026**, plus Cleveland Golf's current official **RTX 6 ZipCore Black Satin** product page checked on **July 15, 2026**. ## Quick Verdict

Buy the **Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore** if you want the smarter current wedge buy for most golfers.

Buy the **Callaway Jaws Raw** if your real goal is simple:

- get serious traditional-wedge performance
- spend a lot less than current-premium money
- and let available loft-and-grind inventory steer the final answer a bit

For most golfers, I would recommend the **RTX 6** first because the live current-model price is still good, the buying path is cleaner, and the wet-condition spin story is one of the best in the category.

For golfers who know exactly what loft and grind they want and are trying to stretch a multi-wedge budget, the **Jaws Raw** is still a hell of a value play.

## Why This Matchup Exists

The **Jaws Raw** and **RTX 6** are not competing on the same emotional pitch.

The **Jaws Raw** pitch is:

"This used to be expensive. Now it is not. Are you smart enough to take the gift?"

The **RTX 6** pitch is:

"You can still buy a current model with real tech and not feel like an idiot."

That makes this less of a pure performance debate and more of a **purchase-quality** debate.

That is useful.

## Price Makes the Jaws Raw Hard to Ignore

This is the whole reason the **Jaws Raw** keeps hanging around in wedge conversations.

The current Birdie Report Jaws Raw inventory work published **July 15, 2026** had **Jaws Raw Face Chrome** pricing from **$53.66 to $133.88**.

That means the Jaws Raw can be:

- far cheaper than the current **RTX 6**
- way cheaper than a new **Vokey SM11**
- and still cheaper than most current players-wedge options even when you shop a nicer condition listing

That matters a lot if you are replacing:

- one worn lob wedge
- a gap-and-sand pair
- or an entire scoring setup

If you need multiple wedges, price is not a small note. It is the entire story.

## Jaws Raw Has the Bigger Matrix, but It Asks More From You

One big reason wedge nerds still like the **Jaws Raw** is the matrix.

The current Jaws Raw coverage in the repo still points to:

- **48 through 62 degrees**
- **C, S, W, X, and Z grinds**
- tungsten in sand and lob wedges
- variable-length hosels
- raw-face and Groove-in-Groove scoring-loft spin tech

That is a serious setup.

But it also means you need to know enough to use it well.

That is the catch.

The **Jaws Raw** is a better deal for informed shoppers.

The **RTX 6** is a better purchase for shoppers who want a strong answer without extra friction.

## Wet Conditions and Everyday Golf: RTX 6 Has the Better Hook

This is where the **RTX 6** gets really annoying in a good way.

Cleveland's current official page does not try to sell you some vague feel mythology. It pushes one practical idea:

**spin from messy lies still matters more than showroom romance**

That is what **HydraZip** and **UltiZip** are built around.

The company specifically frames the striking areas and laser patterns around different loft groupings and consistent spin in **wet or dry** conditions.

That is a real golfer problem.

Most golfers do not play every wedge shot off baked-out tour turf. They play:

- dew
- soft fairways
- damp rough
- spring mud nonsense

That makes the **RTX 6** easier to defend as the better all-around current choice.

## Jaws Raw Still Has the Stronger Bargain-Spin Story

That does not mean the **Jaws Raw** lacks bite.

The **raw face**, **Offset Groove-in-Groove** texture in scoring lofts, and the overall traditional shape still make it a very believable spin-first wedge for golfers who like more shotmaking freedom than cavity-back wedges offer.

And because it is now living in that discount lane, the question changes from:

"Is this the best wedge in the world?"

to:

"Is this enough wedge for way less money?"

That is a much easier question to answer.

For a lot of golfers, the answer is yes.
