This is one of those golf-ball comparisons where the label "soft premium" hides two very different jobs.

The **Bridgestone Tour B RXS** is the premium moderate-speed ball for golfers who want **soft feel and additional spin**.

The **Titleist AVX** is the premium specialist for golfers who want **very soft feel, lower flight, and lower long-game spin**.

Those are not the same golfer.

This comparison is **research-based and feedback-based**, built from the current official **Bridgestone Tour B RXS** and **Titleist AVX** product pages checked on **Monday, July 20, 2026**, plus the surrounding Birdie Report ball cluster. No fake "I solved this in a private bay with six launch monitors and a transcendental short game" nonsense.

One current-buying note matters immediately:

- **Titleist AVX** was listed at **$50/dozen** and showed as **in stock** on Titleist's U.S. product page when checked on **July 20, 2026**
- **Bridgestone Tour B RXS** was listed at **$54.99/dozen** on Bridgestone's direct shop but showed as **unavailable** at the same check

That does not decide the fit by itself, but it absolutely affects the practical recommendation.

## Quick Verdict

Buy the **Titleist AVX** if you already know your ball flight is high enough, your long-game spin could come down, and you want the softer premium ball that intentionally calms everything down.

Buy the **Tour B RXS** if you swing **under 105 mph**, want **additional spin**, and care more about a moderate-speed premium fit than the cleaner current direct-buy path.

For most golfers comparing these two **right now**, I would lean **AVX**.

Not because the **RXS** is bad.

Because the **AVX** currently has:

- the cleaner in-stock checkout
- the slightly cheaper price
- and the sharper specialist case

## Why the RXS Has a Real Case

The **Tour B RXS** matters because Bridgestone actually narrows the buyer.

It is for golfers who:

- swing **under 105 mph**
- want **soft feel**
- want **additional spin**
- still want real premium-ball distance and control

That is useful because not every golfer shopping soft premium balls wants the ball to fly lower and spin less.

Some golfers want the exact opposite.

They want the ball to stay premium while giving them a little more short-game personality and a more believable fit for normal swing speed.

That is the **RXS**.

If that description already sounds like your game, the Bridgestone case gets much stronger immediately.

## Why AVX Is the Cleaner Specialist Buy

The **AVX** is one of the rare Titleist balls that actually tells you who should not buy it.

Titleist positions it around:

- **lower ball flight off the tee**
- **lower long-game spin**
- **very soft feel**
- **excellent greenside control**

That is a much more defined profile than a generic premium flagship.

The **AVX** is not the ball for golfers begging for more launch help.

It is the ball for golfers who want the ball to calm down a little without giving up premium feel.

That is why this matchup leans **AVX** for me unless the under-105 Bridgestone fit is very clearly your thing.

## Feel: Soft vs Very Soft

This sounds minor until you putt with the thing.

The **RXS** is the **soft** premium ball.

The **AVX** is the **very soft** premium ball.

That matters because golfers absolutely notice:

- putter sound
- chip feedback
- how muted a full swing feels

If you want the softer, more muted premium sensation, the **AVX** has the edge.

If you want soft feel but not the softest personality in the room, the **RXS** sits in a more middle-ground premium lane.

Neither is automatically right.

But the golfer who loves the AVX feel profile usually knows it fast.

## Flight and Spin: This Is the Whole Decision

This is where the two balls stop pretending to overlap.

### Choose RXS if your real priority is:

- under-105 swing-speed fit
- added spin and control
- soft premium feel without an aggressive low-flight brief

### Choose AVX if your real priority is:

- lower flight
- lower long-game spin
- very soft feel
- a calmer, more penetrating premium flight

That is it.

If your actual problem is "I already launch it high enough and my long-game spin is still a little busy," the **AVX** is the better answer.

If your actual problem is "I want a premium ball that feels softer and more spin-friendly for my normal-person swing speed," the **RXS** is the better answer.
