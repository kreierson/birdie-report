Into-grain chips make decent golfers look like they have never held a wedge before.

The club slows down.
The ball comes out dead.
And then your brain reacts by trying to lift the next one with your hands like a man panicking at a garage sale.

That is how a normal missed green becomes two more shots and a fresh little argument with your scorecard.

If your bigger problem is choosing between putter and wedge in the first place, start with [when to putt from off the green](/blog/when-to-putt-from-off-the-green/). If the lie is more rough than collar, go straight to [how to chip from greenside rough](/blog/how-to-chip-from-greenside-rough/). This page is for the specific little bastard where the ball is playable, the grass is grabbing, and the shot keeps dying short.

## What Into-Grain Actually Does

When the grain is growing back into you, the grass wants to:

- grab the clubhead more
- reduce speed through impact
- take spin and rollout off the shot
- make the ball come out softer and shorter than your eyes expect

That means the default amateur mistake is obvious:

- they pick too much loft
- land it too close to the fringe
- and leave the ball either in the collar or barely on the green

The fix is not "hit it harder."

The fix is:

- less loft when possible
- an earlier landing spot
- and a slightly firmer, cleaner strike

## My Default Rule: One Less Loft, One Yard Earlier

This is the cleanest into-grain rule I know:

**Use one less loft than your first instinct and land the ball about 1 yard earlier than your normal chip.**

Examples:

- normal shot is a **sand wedge**: check whether a **gap wedge or pitching wedge** can do the job
- normal landing spot is **4 paces on**: move it back to about **3 paces on**
- normal rollout expectation is **two-thirds roll**: assume a little less and choose the flatter option accordingly

Why it works:

- less loft helps the ball fight through the sticky first bounce
- earlier landing keeps you from needing miracle release
- a simpler chip motion beats trying to float a dead little spinner through grabby grass

This is the same grown-up logic behind [chip-shot technique around the green](/blog/chip-shot-technique-around-the-green/) and [how to stop chunking chip shots](/blog/how-to-stop-chunking-chip-shots/). Fewer moving parts. Smaller disaster.

## The Club Ladder I Want You Using

Start with the simplest club that can clear the ugly stuff.

| Lie and shot | First club to consider | What I expect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Light collar, 6-12 feet on green before the hole | **9-iron or PW** | Ball gets moving, checks a little, still releases |
| Slightly thicker collar, moderate carry | **PW or GW** | Safer first bounce, enough loft without getting cute |
| Grainy first cut, little green to work with | **GW or SW** | More carry, but still a boring stock chip |
| Thick grain plus forced carry or front pin | **SW only if lie supports it** | Emergency loft, not your default personality |

If your first answer is always lob wedge, you are making this shot harder on purpose.

## The Landing-Spot Numbers That Clean This Up Fast

Into grain, I want you thinking in paces onto the green, not "somewhere near the hole."

### Plenty of green: 12 or more feet

- land it **2-3 paces on**
- use the lower club that still clears the fringe
- accept rollout, just less than normal

### Medium runway: 6-12 feet

- land it **1-2 paces on**
- favor **PW, GW, or a simple SW**
- do not let the landing spot creep too close to the edge

### Tiny runway: under 6 feet

- this is where you earn the extra loft
- land it on the **first flat safe patch**, usually **1 pace on or less**
- if the lie is iffy, stop chasing perfect and just get it pin-high-ish

Into grain, the bad miss is usually short.

So when you are between two landing spots, I want the slightly farther, safer one unless long is completely dead.

## The Setup That Keeps the Club Moving

My basic setup:

- ball **slightly back of center**
- **60 percent** pressure on the lead foot
- handle just a little ahead
- stance narrow
- chest staying over the ball

That setup helps the club keep moving without adding some weird scoop move at the bottom.

Important detail:

**I want a firm little chest-driven motion, not a hand-flip.**

If the grain is slowing the club, your instinct will be to help the ball up.
Do not.

That adds loft, adds bounce at the wrong time, and usually gives you the dead little pop-up that travels nowhere.

## The Three Mistakes That Keep Showing Up

### 1. Too much loft for no reason

Golfers see sticky grass and think they need more height.

Usually they need the opposite.

More loft often means:

- more speed lost through impact
- less rollout than expected
- and a shorter shot than the picture in your head

### 2. Landing it too close to the fringe

This is probably the biggest one.

You are afraid of running it by, so you try to land it barely on.
Into grain, that is how the ball grabs, stalls, and leaves you another chip.

### 3. Slowing down through the strike

The motion should feel:

- shorter
- tidier
- and slightly more positive through impact

Not violent.
Just not scared.

## When to Putt Instead

If the grainy section is short, predictable, and not especially thick, putting can still win.

Use the putter when:

- the collar is thin enough that the ball should roll through it
- the shot does not need carry
- and the green beyond it is not screaming downhill chaos

If the collar is thick, sticky, or uphill into the grain, I lean chip because the putt can die before it ever starts acting like a real putt.

That is also why this page pairs well with [save more pars after missed greens](/blog/save-more-pars-after-missed-greens/). Choosing the less stupid option is most of scrambling.

## The 12-Ball Into-Grain Drill

Drop **12 balls** in the same into-grain collar or first-cut spot.

Hit:

- **4 with 9-iron or PW**
- **4 with GW**
- **4 with SW**

Track only two things:

- how many finish inside **6 feet**
- how many fail to get at least **3 feet onto the green**

Benchmarks:

- **7 or more inside 6 feet** is solid
- **2 or fewer** dying before the green is acceptable
- if **3 or more** die in the collar, your loft or landing spot is wrong

This drill is useful because it shows you very quickly whether you are choosing too much loft for the amount of grain.

## The 9-Ball Landing-Spot Test

Put three towels or headcovers down:

- one at **1 pace on**
- one at **2 paces on**
- one at **3 paces on**

Then hit:

- **3 low chips**
- **3 standard chips**
- **3 slightly lofted chips**

Scoring:

- **2 points** if the first bounce lands within one pace of the intended spot
- **1 point** if the ball finishes inside **8 feet**
- **0 points** if it finishes short of the green

Good score: **12 or better out of 18**

If your first bounce is inconsistent, your finish distance will always feel random.

## The Up-and-Down Reality Drill

Pick **6 into-grain spots** around a practice green.

Play one ball from each and putt out.

Track:

- up and downs
- bogey or better
- chips that never reached a makeable second putt range

Targets:

- **2 of 6** up and downs is solid for a normal amateur
- **5 of 6** bogey or better means the decisions are sane
- **0 second chips** is the real win

That last number matters most.

An into-grain chip does not need to be sexy.
It needs to stop being a double-bogey audition.

## My On-Course Rule

If I see grain into me, I ask:

1. can I use less loft?
2. where is the first landing spot that gets the ball properly onto the green?
3. what shot removes the chance of leaving it in the collar?

Then I hit the simplest answer.

That usually means a lower chip, landed a little earlier, with a little more conviction.

Not some floating mini-flop because my ego got bored.

## Bottom Line

Into-grain chips get easier when you stop treating them like normal chips.

Use:

- **one less loft**
- **one yard earlier landing**
- **a firmer chest-driven motion**
- and **practice that tracks how often you actually get the ball onto the green**

Do that, and this shot stops being the annoying little short-game tax it has probably been charging you.

It becomes what it should be:

a boring chip that gets on the green and lets you move on with your life.
