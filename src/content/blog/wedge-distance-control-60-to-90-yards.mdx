The **60-90 yard** wedge zone should be free money.

Instead, a lot of golfers turn it into a full-contact argument with their own nerves.

They get a perfect layup number, grab a wedge, then do one of three dumb things:

- swing too softly and leave it short
- swing too hard and fly it over everything
- or try to invent a magical feel shot they have never practiced on purpose

That is not a talent problem.

That is a system problem.

This is the gap between [40-60 yard wedge control](/blog/how-to-control-wedges-40-to-60-yards/) and [110-124 yard approach strategy](/blog/approach-strategy-110-124-yards/). It is also the scoring band where decent golfers can start separating themselves fast.

## The Real Job From 60-90 Yards

You are not trying to hit a television wedge.

You are trying to:

- carry a safe number with margin
- land it in a predictable window
- finish inside about **20 feet** when you hit it well
- and eliminate the automatic bogey that comes from missing short-sided or short of the green

That means you need stock carries, not vibes.

## Build One Ladder, Not Nine Random Feel Shots

Most golfers need fewer wedge motions, not more.

Start with a **three-swing ladder**:

| Swing | Feel | Typical finish | Main use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **9:00** | lead arm to parallel | chest-high finish | **60-70 yards** |
| **10:30** | hands just above trail shoulder | shoulder-high finish | **72-82 yards** |
| **11:00** | three-quarter swing | full balanced finish | **84-90 yards** |

Then map those three swings to two wedges.

For a very normal mid-handicap setup:

| Carry | Club | Swing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **62-66** | 58° / 56° | 9:00 |
| **68-72** | 54° / 52° | 9:00 |
| **74-78** | 58° / 56° | 10:30 |
| **80-84** | 54° / 52° | 10:30 |
| **86-90** | 58° / 56° | 11:00 or stronger wedge 10:30 |

Your numbers will vary. Fine.

The important part is that you stop letting every yardage become its own emotional event.

## My Default Setup Numbers

Start with this:

- **55 to 60 percent** pressure on your lead foot
- ball **one ball back of center**
- feet slightly narrower than full-swing wedge stance
- grip pressure around **4 out of 10**
- chest and sternum staying over the ball, not drifting behind it

That setup gives you a cleaner strike window and makes it a lot harder to flip the club at the bottom.

If you want more scoring-zone context first, read [pitch-shot distance control](/blog/pitch-shot-distance-control-scoring-zone/) and [how to practice with purpose](/blog/how-to-practice-with-purpose/). Partial wedges get dramatically better once you stop treating them like improvised mini full swings.

## The One Rule That Cleans Up Decision-Making Fast

Here is the rule:

**Choose the shot that carries the front safe number by at least 3 yards without requiring your absolute prettiest swing.**

Example:

- front safe number is **67**
- flag is **74**
- your stock 56-degree 9:00 carries **65**
- your stock 52-degree 9:00 carries **71**

The answer is obvious.

Take the **52-degree 9:00** and stop pretending the weaker-club hero shot is smarter just because it looks cuter in your head.

This is the same adult logic from [how to play front pins without making bogey](/blog/how-to-play-front-pins-without-making-bogey/) and [how to stop short-siding yourself](/blog/stop-short-siding-yourself-approach-strategy/). Carry safety first. Flag-hunting second.

## What Good Contact Should Feel Like

For these shots, I want:

- quiet lower body
- committed chest rotation
- shallow brush after the ball
- and one tempo for every swing length

The strike should feel like a firm, boring nip.

Not scoopy.
Not violent.
Not decelerated because your brain suddenly remembered water exists.

If you are hitting these fat, it is usually one of three things:

- ball too far forward
- hanging back trying to help it up
- or changing speed instead of changing swing length

The fix is almost never "try harder."

## The Three Misses That Wreck This Yardage Band

### 1. Leaving it short because the pin looked scary

This is the classic.

Golfers say they were being conservative.

No, they were being scared.

Conservative is hitting the middle with the right carry.
Scared is hitting the bunker lip with the wrong one.

### 2. Adding speed late

You get nervous about leaving it short, then rush the downswing and hit a jumper.

That is why the ladder matters. The backswing length decides the shot. The tempo stays the same.

### 3. Choosing a lofted shot when a flatter one is easier

From **78** with plenty of green, you do not need the romantic high spinner.

You need the shot that lands around **6-10 yards** on and releases a little.

The flatter stock shot is usually the smarter play.

## The 15-Ball Ladder Drill

This is the fastest practice block I know for this range.

Pick five carries:

- **62**
- **68**
- **74**
- **82**
- **88**

Hit three balls to each.

Rules:

- call the club and swing length before every shot
- one point if the carry finishes within **3 yards**
- one point if the ball finishes inside **20 feet**
- zero points if it comes up short of the safe carry number

Maximum score: **30**

Benchmarks:

- **22+** means you have a real scoring pattern
- **17-21** means playable but loose
- **16 or worse** means you are still mostly freelancing

## The 9-Ball Randomizer

Write down nine random carries between **60** and **90**.

Example:

- **61**
- **69**
- **73**
- **80**
- **66**
- **88**
- **75**
- **83**
- **70**

Hit one ball to each.

Track:

- intended carry
- actual carry
- whether the miss was short, long, left, or right

If the pattern is mostly short, your ladder is under-clubbed.
If the pattern is mostly long, your adrenaline is leaking into the motion.
If the pattern is random, your wedge matrix is fictional.

## The On-Course Checkpoints I Want

For your next five rounds, track every wedge from **60-90 yards**.

Write down:

- yardage
- club
- swing length
- result

The checkpoints:

- average leave under **24 feet**
- at least **half** of them pin-high or past the front safe number
- zero short-sides caused by choosing the wrong flight

If you are missing every other one short, your practice is lying to you.

## Where I Actually Aim

From this range, I am usually aiming:

- middle of the green on front pins
- pin-high center on middle pins
- one pace short of back-third pins unless the number is perfect

That is because I would rather hit a boring **18-footer** than manufacture a heroic short-sided chip from a distance that should have been a birdie look.

If your layup game keeps feeding you bad wedge numbers, clean up [how to lay up on par 5s](/blog/how-to-lay-up-on-par-5s/) and [how to play short par 4s without dumb bogeys](/blog/how-to-play-short-par-4s-without-dumb-bogeys/). The wedge system only works if the setup shot is not stupid.

## Bottom Line

The **60-90 yard** wedge zone gets easier the second you stop asking feel to do all the work.

Build:

- **three swing lengths**
- **two wedges you trust**
- and **stock carry windows** you can repeat without panic

That is enough to turn this from an awkward half-shot range into one of the best scoring bands in your bag.

And if you still keep flying one to **97** because you got greedy and jabby, at least now you will know exactly which part of the process to blame.
