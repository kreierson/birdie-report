This is one of the few wedge matchups where the obvious premium option is not automatically the smarter buy.

The **Titleist Vokey SM11** is the benchmark wedge family. That part is not up for debate.

The **Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore** is the wedge that keeps asking an annoying but important question:

Why are you paying extra if the cheaper one might fit the way you actually play golf better?

This comparison is **research-based** and built from the current official Titleist and Cleveland product pages checked on **May 22, 2026**. Titleist's current U.S. product pages show **Vokey SM11** starting at **$199** in Tour Chrome, Nickel, and Jet Black finishes, with **six grinds**, **27 total configurations**, and lofts from **44 to 60 degrees**. Cleveland's current U.S. product pages show **RTX 6 ZipCore Black Satin** at **$139.99**, with **HydraZip**, **UltiZip grooves**, **ZipCore**, and four core sole options built around different turf and delivery needs.

## Quick Verdict

Buy the **Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore** if you want the smarter wedge buy for most real golfers in **2026**.

Buy the **Titleist Vokey SM11** if you are the kind of golfer who will actually use the deeper fitting matrix and cares enough about sole nuance to justify paying for it.

My lean:

- **RTX 6 ZipCore** for value, wet-condition spin, and easier self-service buying
- **Vokey SM11** for the golfer getting fit or the wedge nerd who already knows exactly what he wants

## Price Is the Whole Reason This Comparison Matters

| | Titleist Vokey SM11 | Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore |
|---|---|---|
| **Current official starting price** | $199 | $139.99 |
| **Lofts** | 44-60 degrees | 48-60 degrees in the current Black Satin lineup |
| **Main identity** | premium fitting depth and premium feel | practical spin value and real-world consistency |
| **Best fit** | golfers who know their wedge setup cold | golfers who want performance without paying prestige tax |

That price gap is not small.

At roughly **$59 per wedge**, the difference becomes very real if you are buying two or three scoring clubs at once.

That means the **SM11** cannot win on "it is nicer." It has to win on "you will actually use what makes it nicer."

## Fitting Depth: Vokey Still Owns This

The cleanest **SM11** advantage is still configuration depth.

Titleist gives you:

- **six grinds**
- **27 total configurations**
- lofts from **44 through 60**
- a lineup designed around keeping launch windows more predictable across sole shapes

That is the best wedge-fitting menu in golf.

If you already know:

- which loft gaps you want
- whether you prefer lower or higher bounce
- how much heel or trailing-edge relief you like
- what kind of turf interaction gives you confidence

then the **SM11** makes more sense.

That is exactly why golfers who take wedge fitting seriously still start with Vokey.

## Simplicity and Self-Service Shopping: Cleveland Wins This

The **RTX 6** is the calmer buy.

Cleveland's wedge line still gives you meaningful choices, but it does not ask the average golfer to become a part-time sole-design philosopher before checkout.

That matters.

Most golfers are not getting fit.

Most golfers are buying a wedge because:

- their grooves are cooked
- they want more spin in bad conditions
- they want a club that behaves better from rough, sand, and wet turf
- they do not want to spend almost two hundred bucks per head unless they have a good reason

The **RTX 6** is easier to recommend in that world.

If your real fork is whether the latest Callaway value pitch is even stronger than Cleveland's, read [Callaway Opus vs Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore](/blog/callaway-opus-vs-cleveland-rtx-6-zipcore-wedges/) next.

## Spin and Conditions: Cleveland Has the Better Real-World Hook

This is where **RTX 6** keeps earning its place.

Cleveland built the line around:

- **HydraZip**
- **UltiZip grooves**
- **ZipCore**

That combination is aimed squarely at one real golfer problem: wedge performance gets weird once the turf is damp, the lie is imperfect, and the strike is not tour-clean.

That is a better problem to solve than a lot of wedge marketing focuses on.

The **SM11** absolutely brings elite spin and control. It is still the cleaner premium-control choice.

But if you play:

- dewy morning golf
- spring and fall golf
- soft fairways
- rough that actually exists

the **RTX 6** is easier to justify because its core story lives in those conditions.

## Feel and Premium Confidence: Vokey Still Gets the Nod

This is the category Cleveland never completely steals.

The **Vokey** case is not just about performance. It is also about trust:

- trust in the look
- trust in the feel
- trust in the fitting ecosystem
- trust that if you are spending real money on scoring clubs, you are buying the category standard

That matters more in wedges than it does in a lot of other equipment.

The **RTX 6** feels solid and capable. The **SM11** still feels like the premium answer for golfers who care deeply about touch and feedback.

That is why the honest recommendation is not "Cleveland is better."

It is "Cleveland is often the better buy."

## Final Verdict

The **Titleist Vokey SM11** are still the premium benchmark.

The **Cleveland RTX 6 ZipCore** are still the smarter buy for a huge percentage of golfers.

That is the answer.

If you are the golfer who will use the extra fitting depth and care about the finer details of bounce, grind, and turf interaction, buy the **SM11** and do it on purpose.

If you want the wedge that solves more normal-golfer problems at a much friendlier price, buy the **RTX 6** and stop pretending prestige saves more shots than fit and value.
