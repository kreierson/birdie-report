Reachable par 5s make golfers stupid.

You see **236 to the front**, convince yourself this is your moment, lash a fairway wood into a pond, and somehow turn the friendliest hole on the property into a rescue operation.

That is not "staying aggressive."

That is just expensive optimism.

The right question on a reachable par 5 is not:

**Can I get there?**

It is:

**Does going for it beat a clean layup and wedge?**

That answer should come from three green lights. If all three are not on, you are usually better off leaving a real number and trying to make birdie the boring way.

If you want the bigger par-5 framework first, start with [how to play par 5s without blowups](/blog/how-to-play-par-5s-without-blowups/) and [how to lay up on par 5s](/blog/how-to-lay-up-on-par-5s/). This piece is the tighter attack-or-back-off decision once the second shot actually looks tempting.

## The Goal Is Not Eagle. The Goal Is a Cheap Birdie Chance

On reachable par 5s, I want you thinking in this order:

1. can I create a putt, fringe putt, or simple up-and-down look without bringing double into play?
2. if not, can I leave **85 to 115 yards** from fairway and attack with a wedge?
3. if not, can I at least avoid the full-card-ruining miss?

That is adult golf.

The scorecard does not give bonus points for "good try with the 5-wood."

## Green Light 1: Your Stock Carry Clears the Front by Real Margin

This is the first filter because it kills most fake opportunities immediately.

My rule:

- if your stock carry does **not** clear the front trouble or front edge by at least **10 yards**, it is not a green light
- if the shot needs **95% or more** of your best-ever carry, it is a yellow at best and usually a red
- if a slight mishit finishes in water, deep bunker, or junk, you are not attacking, you are gambling

Example:

- front edge: **228**
- water starts: **221**
- your normal 5-wood carry: **231 to 234**

That is thin.

You technically can get there.
You do not cover the front with enough comfort to call it a smart attack.

Now change the math:

- front edge: **219**
- front trouble ends: **213**
- your normal 5-wood carry: **231 to 234**

That is a real green light. You have enough carry buffer that a normal strike still gives the shot a chance.

If the hole is drifting into true long-club territory, pair this with [the 225-plus yard approach guide](/blog/approach-strategy-225-plus-yards/). Same honesty. Same front-edge rule. Slightly meaner consequences.

## Green Light 2: The Lie and Wind Support Launch, Not Hope

A clean number means nothing if the shot conditions are trash.

Ask these before you try to get home:

- is the ball sitting clean enough to launch a fairway wood or hybrid?
- is the wind neutral or helping a little, not punching you in the teeth?
- can you make a normal swing without trying to nuke it?

Bad attack conditions:

- ball sitting down in rough
- flyer lie that can jump long or come out dead
- ball below your feet
- heavy headwind
- wet turf where thin contact is lurking

If two of those show up, I am laying up almost automatically.

That is not conservative. That is just refusing to ask a bad lie for a heroic answer.

This is the same basic honesty behind [how to hit fairway woods and hybrids off the deck](/blog/how-to-hit-fairway-woods-hybrids-off-the-deck/) and [how to play flyer lies without airmailing greens](/blog/how-to-play-flyer-lies-without-airmailing-greens/). Some shots are green-light swings. Others are just dressed-up recovery shots.

## Green Light 3: The Miss Has To Be Cheap Enough

This is the filter golfers ignore because it forces them to admit the hole is not actually set up for attack.

When you go for a par 5 in two, I want at least one miss that still leaves the hole alive.

Good cheap misses:

- front fringe with room to chip or putt
- greenside bunker you would accept from that side
- one safe rough side with green to work with
- long but playable if long is not dead

Bad miss maps:

- water short and bunker long
- trees right and penalty left
- front bunker with a vertical lip and a short-sided pin
- false front that sends everything back 20 yards

If the shot has **two scorecard-killing misses**, it is not a green light. It is fake temptation.

That is where [recovery-shot strategy](/blog/recovery-shot-strategy-stop-doubles/) starts mattering before you have even swung. The best recovery shot is the one you never need because you laid up instead of trying to win an argument with geometry.

## The 30-Second Reachable Par-5 Check

If the second shot looks close enough to matter, run this quick script:

1. **What must carry?**
Front edge, water line, bunker lip, whatever actually matters.

2. **What is my normal carry with this club today?**
Not range-hero carry. Normal carry.

3. **What happens on a slight miss?**
Thin, heel, little block, little pull. Where do those go?

4. **What is my favorite layup number if this is not green?**
Usually **90 to 115 yards** for most golfers.

5. **Does going for it clearly beat a wedge from fairway?**
If the answer is "maybe," that usually means no.

That whole check should take about as long as the average golfer spends pretending the sprinkler head was blurry.

## My Simple Attack Rule

Go for it only when all three are true:

- you have at least **10 yards** of comfortable front-carry margin
- the lie and wind support a normal launch
- there is at least one cheap miss

If one is missing, I am yellow.
If two are missing, I am laying up.

That is the rule.

Could you still pull off the shot on a yellow?
Sure.

You can also make a 35-footer with your eyes half closed sometimes. That does not make it the smart baseline plan.

## The Wedge Question Most Golfers Hate

Let us be blunt.

A lot of golfers are worse from a sketchy greenside miss than they are from **98 yards in the fairway**.

That matters.

If going for it gives you:

- fringe at best
- short-side bunker at worst
- rough over the green on the common miss

...and laying up gives you **104 yards** from fairway, the wedge route is often the better birdie play.

This is exactly why [wedge distance control from 90 to 120 yards](/blog/wedge-distance-control-90-to-120-yards/) and [60 to 90 yards](/blog/wedge-distance-control-60-to-90-yards/) matter so much. Reachable par 5s are not only power holes. They are decision holes.

## What Different Golfers Should Actually Do

### If you are trying to break 100

Do not chase eagles.

Your reachable par-5 job is:

- keep the tee ball in play
- advance the second shot sensibly
- leave a full wedge
- get the third on the green

Birdie is a bonus.
Bogey avoidance is the real win.

### If you are trying to break 90

Attack only when the shot is fully green.

If it is yellow, lay up.
That single discipline change usually saves at least one stupid bogey or double every couple rounds.

### If you are trying to break 80

Now the attack decision matters more, but it still has to be earned.

You should know:

- your actual fairway-wood and hybrid carry windows
- which misses around a par-5 green you can handle
- your favorite layup number
- and whether your fairway lie changes the club choice

If you do not know those, you are still playing lottery golf.

## The 6-Ball Attack-or-Layup Drill

This is the fastest way to make the on-course decision less emotional.

Bring:

- one fairway wood or hybrid you would actually try from a reachable par 5
- one layup club
- one wedge for your favorite scoring number

Run six-ball sets:

1. Pick a target that represents a front edge or front hazard.
2. Hit **3 attack balls** and track how many would have cleared by at least **10 yards**.
3. Hit **3 layup balls** to a number between **90 and 115 yards**.
4. Finish with **3 wedges** to that layup target.

Scoring:

- attack ball only counts if it would have carried the front by **10 yards** and stayed inside your safe miss corridor
- layup ball only counts if it finishes inside a **10-yard window**
- wedge only counts if it finishes inside **25 feet**

Benchmarks I like:

- at least **2 of 3** attack balls qualify before you even think about using that club aggressively on the course
- at least **2 of 3** layups finish in your planned window
- at least **2 of 3** wedges finish inside **25 feet**

If the attack club fails that test constantly, the course is not the place to discover it.

## The Scorecard Numbers to Track

For your next five rounds, track this on every reachable par 5:

- did you go or lay up?
- did the decision match the three-green-light filter?
- did you make par or better?
- did you make bogey or worse?

Good early benchmarks:

- **par or better on at least half** of reachable par 5s
- **bogey or worse on no more than 1 of every 5** reachable par 5s
- zero penalty strokes from second-shot hero attempts

If the last number is not zero, the decision process still stinks.

## Bottom Line

Going for a par 5 in two is smart only when:

- your stock carry clears the front by real margin
- the lie and wind support a normal launch
- and the miss still leaves the hole alive

If one of those is off, lay up to a number you actually like and go make birdie with a wedge instead of a prayer.

That is less cinematic.

It is also how golfers stop turning reachable par 5s into sixes.
