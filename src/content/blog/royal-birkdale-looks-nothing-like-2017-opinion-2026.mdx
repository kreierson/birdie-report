The best thing **Royal Birkdale** could do this week was stop cosplaying as **2017**.

And from everything coming out of the course, it looks like that is exactly what it has done.

According to **Associated Press reporting published July 12, 2026** and **The Open's official course-change and hole-by-hole guides**, checked on **July 14, 2026**, Birkdale now has a **241-yard par-3 15th**, a redesigned **risk-reward fifth**, a reworked **par-5 14th**, and a much nastier **18th tee shot**, all on turf AP described as more **brown than green** after a hot spell in Europe.

Good.

This column is based on **AP's July 12 Royal Birkdale preview** plus **The Open's official course guides** checked on **July 14, 2026**. No pretending I was out there hitting low chasers off baked fescue with a notebook and a weather station.

## 2017 Memory Is Not Going To Save Anybody

AP's Birkdale preview made the important point bluntly: this place does not really play like the version people remember from **Jordan Spieth's** 2017 win.

AP said the old driving-range area from Spieth's famous **13th-hole** chaos is now part of a **fan village** and out of bounds, while the old **par-3 14th** is gone and the hole order around the closing stretch has changed. The same report said **Peter Uihlein** got to the new **15th** and basically reacted like a man who had arrived at the wrong golf course.

Perfect.

Nostalgia is fine until it starts flattening the actual test.

If players have to show up with old yardage-book memories and then realize the angles, carry windows, and decision points are different, that is a feature. The final men's major of the year should not feel like a museum exhibit with fresh hospitality.

## The R&A Actually Changed the Right Stuff

The official **Open** materials are useful here because they show this was not random tinkering.

The course-change page says:

- the **5th** is now a fully redesigned short **par 4** with a visible green and a more dangerous go-for-it option
- the old **15th** is now the **14th**, a long **par 5** with tighter layup demands
- the new **15th** is the **longest par 3 on the course**
- and the **18th** now starts from a tee moved well left, turning the drive into a straighter, more bunker-heavy problem

That is thoughtful violence.

The official hole guide adds useful details. It says the **15th** plays **241 yards**, the green only looks tiny from the tee because the entrance is narrow, and the surface runs **front to back**, which means balls can keep feeding away when the wind gets up. It also says the new **5th** still tempts players to take a rip, but the smarter play is often to lay back around **200 yards** and wedge it from there.

In other words, Birkdale should now force more decisions and punish more ego.

Again: good.

## The Bigger Story Might Just Be the Turf

The architectural changes matter.

The ground might matter even more.

AP reported the course was looking more yellow than green and quoted **Joe Greiner**, caddie for **Akshay Bhatia**, comparing the feel to **Carnoustie 2018** because of how much the ball could run. AP also relayed **Uihlein's** point that loft starts meaning less when it gets this firm and windy.

That is the kind of Open setup I want.

We already saw hints of this in Scotland when **Rory McIlroy** was talking about a [new wedge for firmer turf in our 36-hole Scottish Open update](/blog/rory-mcilroy-tom-kim-jordan-smith-scottish-open-lead-2026/). Now the championship venue itself looks ready to lean harder into that style of golf.

If players have to land approaches absurdly short, rethink stock numbers, and treat the ground like a second opponent, then the tournament is doing its job.

## This Also Makes the Contender Board More Honest

One reason I like the setup story is that it should strip some fake certainty off the preview models.

Yes, **Scottie Scheffler** is still the best player in the world.
Yes, **Rory McIlroy** is still the biggest name in the week.
Yes, **Tom Kim**, **Tommy Fleetwood**, **Jordan Spieth**, and half a dozen others all come with their own hooks.

But a firmer, faster, more unfamiliar Birkdale means the tournament should be a little less about prestige memory and a little more about who adapts quickest from **Thursday, July 16, 2026** onward.

That is healthier than handing everyone a 2017 highlight reel and asking them to pretend it counts as current prep.

## My Take

I do not want a major venue preserved in amber.

I want it alive enough to make the best players solve new problems under pressure.

If **Royal Birkdale** now asks for different tee shots, different landing spots, and a lot more humility on firm links turf, then the **R&A** got this one right.

## Bottom Line

**Royal Birkdale** heading into **The 154th Open** looks firmer, faster, and structurally different enough from **2017** that old memory should not carry much value.

According to **AP's July 12 preview** and **The Open's official course guides** checked on **July 14, 2026**, that means a new **241-yard par-3 15th**, a more dangerous **5th**, a harsher **18th**, and exactly the kind of uncomfortable links test this championship should want.
