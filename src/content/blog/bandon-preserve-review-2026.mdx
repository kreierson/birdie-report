There is a lazy way to talk about **Bandon Preserve**.

It goes something like this:

"Cute little par-3. Nice views. Good for a loosen-up round."

That undersells the hell out of it.

**Bandon Preserve** looks more like the round that keeps a Bandon trip from turning into a joyless 36-hole machismo contest. It is the course that lets you see the coast, hit a pile of scoring clubs, and still have enough legs left to enjoy dinner like a functioning adult.

This is not a fake firsthand review where I pretend I just walked off the 13th green with a poetic relationship to every wedge yardage on property. This is a practical review built from Bandon Dunes' current official **Bandon Preserve** course page, current **2026** green-fee page, and current resort FAQ checked on **July 17, 2026**.

The question is simple:

**Is Bandon Preserve actually worth planning around in 2026, or is it just the thing people play because their room is not ready yet?**

Yes, it looks worth planning around.

And for a lot of trips, it may be the smartest first move on property.

## Quick Verdict

**Bandon Preserve is worth it** if you want:

- a **13-hole par-3** round that still feels like real Bandon golf instead of novelty filler
- a lower-cost way to get ocean views, firm ground, and wind decisions without another full 18-hole beating
- an arrival-day or late-day round that adds fun without wrecking the rest of the trip
- a short-course stop that still rewards wedge control, trajectory, and lag putting

It is not the move if you only care about max brag-value or if you think anything under full-length championship golf is automatically a side dish.

## What Bandon Preserve Actually Is

Bandon's current official course page describes **Bandon Preserve** as a **13-hole par-3 course** with spectacular views, says it opened in **2012**, and says it was designed by **Bill Coore and Ben Crenshaw**.

That is already enough to make the course matter.

The current official description also says the routing begins high atop a sand dune and works down toward the beach. That sounds exactly like what a Bandon short course should be:

- exposed
- scenic
- a little mischievous
- and still interested in asking golf questions instead of just handing you postcard holes

## The Practical Stuff That Matters

### The current rates are separate, and that matters

One important correction if you have older Bandon assumptions in your head:

the current official **2026** green-fee page lists **Bandon Preserve** as its own paid round with separate pricing. So no, this is not a free little side toy bundled invisibly into the trip.

That is worth knowing before you budget the week like a maniac.

### It is still walking-only, because of course it is

Bandon's current official green-fee page says **all courses are walking only**.

The current FAQ also says:

- complimentary **pull/push carts** are available
- caddies are encouraged
- caddies cost **$125 per bag, per round plus gratuity**

On Preserve specifically, I think the free pull-cart option is the sane play for most people. Save the caddie spend for one of the full-length strategy monsters unless your group just wants the full Bandon ritual everywhere.

### Aerification timing still matters

Bandon's current FAQ says any course played within **five days of aerification** gets a **25% discount off the greens fee**.

For **Bandon Preserve**, the current **2026** aerification windows listed are:

- **May 12-13, 2026**
- **October 26-27, 2026**

That is useful if you are price-sensitive.

It is also useful if you are not price-sensitive and would rather avoid compromised short-game greens on the exact course where wedge control and putting feel are supposed to be the fun part.

### Golf-only booking terms can still bite you

Bandon's current FAQ says:

- **golf-only reservations** require deposit payment for the rounds reserved at booking
- and golf-only reservations become **non-refundable inside 90 days**

That is a very adult detail.

If Preserve is part of a golf-only stop rather than a full resort stay, do not drift past that window because your buddy "still needs to check one work thing."

## How I Would Actually Use the Round

### 1. Treat it as a wedge-distance audit

Preserve should tell you pretty quickly whether your **80- to 130-yard** game is organized or mostly vibes.

Before the trip, make sure you have at least three stock windows you trust:

- one carry number around **80 to 90 yards**
- one around **95 to 110 yards**
- one around **115 to 130 yards**

If you do not, go fix that with [wedge distance control from 60 to 90 yards](/blog/wedge-distance-control-60-to-90-yards/) and [how to control 30- to 40-yard pitch shots](/blog/how-to-control-30-to-40-yard-pitch-shots/).

### 2. Keep the ball below the hole whenever you can

Short-course golf gets stupid fast when golfers decide every pin is a dartboard invitation.

On a scenic par-3 course, the smarter checkpoint is simple:

- if you are between clubs, take the one that leaves the safer miss under the hole
- if the wind is weird, favor the center over the selfie line

That is boring advice.

It also travels much better than downhill three-putt speeches.

### 3. Use the round to settle your trip pace

Preserve looks like the place to calm down.

Walk.
Hit shots.
Get the ball on the ground.
Relearn that Bandon wind changes club selection.

If you arrive trying to force immediate hero golf, the course probably still rules. You just may learn less from it.
