Ball-below-your-feet lies are where a lot of perfectly decent rounds start leaking dumb shots.

The ball looks reachable.
The yardage looks fine.
Then you hit some weak little wipe that never really covers the front and finishes right of where you were even willing to be wrong.

That is not random.

This lie usually does three things:

- makes centered contact harder
- makes the ball want to leak right
- and tempts you to stand up through impact because reaching the ball feels annoying

If you want the broad version first, read [how to hit from uneven lies in golf](/blog/how-to-hit-from-uneven-lies-golf/) and [how to play sidehill lies without double-crossing it](/blog/how-to-play-sidehill-lies-without-double-crossing-it/). This page is the narrower follow-up for the specific sidehill shot that keeps turning makeable approaches into weak apologies.

## The Goal Is Not Perfect. The Goal Is Predictable.

On a ball-below-your-feet lie, I am not asking for art.

I am asking for:

- solid enough contact
- the correct start line
- and a miss that does not immediately create bogey stress

That means you need a system, not hope.

## My Default Setup for This Lie

This is my starting baseline:

- choke down **0.5 to 1 inch**
- add a little knee flex
- widen your stance just a touch
- keep your chest over the ball
- feel pressure through the **middle of your feet**, not out on your toes

That setup gives you a chance to reach the ball without lunging toward it.

Lunging is the whole disaster here.

Once your chest rises and your arms throw at it, you get the classic garbage outcomes:

- thin strike
- low-face strike
- wipey cut
- weak shot that comes up short and right

## The First Rule: Move the Target Left Before You Swing

The lie already wants to move the ball right for a lot of golfers.

So stop aiming at the flag like the hill owes you fairness.

My baseline start-line shift:

- **5 to 8 yards left** with wedges and short irons
- **8 to 12 yards left** with mid-irons
- more if the slope is severe and your stock miss already hangs right

That is your starting point.

Not your forever law.

But if you are currently aiming at the pin and acting shocked when the ball leaks right, this one adjustment cleans up a stupid amount of damage immediately.

If the pin is already tucked right, or the miss right is dead, use the same adult strategy from [how to play front pins without making bogey](/blog/how-to-play-front-pins-without-making-bogey/), [how to play middle pins better](/blog/how-to-play-middle-pins-better/), and [stop short-siding yourself](/blog/stop-short-siding-yourself-approach-strategy/).

In other words:

hit the fat side and move on with your life.

## The Second Rule: Ask Whether the Front Number Is Still Safe

This lie is not only directional.

It also messes with strike quality.

That means the ball can come out a little weak even when the swing does not feel terrible.

So before every full shot, ask:

**Can I still cover the front edge with my normal club from this lie?**

If the answer is "eh, probably" or "if I really flush it," that usually means:

- take **one more club**
- make the same tempo swing
- and stop pretending you are about to hit the one perfect strike this lie has been begging for all day

That matters even more when the green sits up, the front is guarded, or the miss short brings gross short-game work. Pair this with [how to play elevated greens without coming up short](/blog/how-to-play-elevated-greens-without-coming-up-short/) and [approach strategy 125-149 yards](/blog/approach-strategy-125-149-yards/) if those are the exact numbers where you keep donating strokes.

## The Third Rule: Cap the Swing at 80 to 85 Percent

This lie punishes violence.

The instinct is usually the opposite.

Golfers feel awkward, so they swing harder to "commit."

That is backwards.

My default is:

- **80 to 85 percent speed**
- balanced finish
- chest staying down through strike

If you cannot hold your finish for a full second, the swing was probably too aggressive for the lie.

## The 4 Checkpoints I Want Before Pulling the Trigger

Before you swing, answer these:

### 1. How much slope is really there?

Mild slope and severe slope are not the same shot.

If it is only a little below your feet, the adjustment can stay modest.
If it is a true hanging sidehill lie, give yourself more left start line and more margin.

### 2. What is the cheap miss?

On most of these shots, I would much rather leave myself:

- a **25-foot putt** from the fat side

than:

- a short-sided chip from the low-right miss

That should guide both target and club.

### 3. Does the front of the green require a perfect strike?

If yes, the pin is usually not the assignment.

The assignment is covering the front and taking the boring putt.

### 4. Can I stay in posture all the way through?

If the answer feels shaky, rehearse the finish before you hit it.

Not the backswing.
The finish.

Feel your chest stay over the shot and your balance stay quiet.

## What Good Looks Like

From this lie, a good shot is usually one that:

- starts on the correct leftward line
- flies with enough height to cover the front
- and finishes somewhere between the center and safe-right section of the green

That is especially true from **130 to 170 yards**, where too many golfers turn a slightly awkward lie into a pin-hunting crime scene.

My scoring expectation on these shots is simple:

- center green is a win
- front-middle green is a win
- fat side of the green is a win
- short-right bunker because I got greedy is not a win

## Common Mistakes That Keep Showing Up

### Standing up through impact

This is the big one.

You feel far from the ball, so you rise.
Once you rise, the club loses the bottom of the arc.
Then you catch it weak, thin, or glancing.

### Aiming at the flag anyway

This is ego dressed as optimism.

The lie already told you the shot wants to leak right.
Believe it.

### Keeping the same club when the front carry is tight

This is how you hit those shots that feel "not awful" and still finish in the exact place you could not afford.

## Drill 1: The 9-Ball Start-Line Drill

Use one sidehill lie with the ball below your feet.

Hit:

- **3 balls with a wedge**
- **3 balls with an 8-iron**
- **3 balls with a 6- or 7-iron**

Pick a target and create a safe start-line window **left** of it.

Scoring:

- **2 points** if the ball starts in the left-window and finishes on the green or intended safe side
- **1 point** if the contact is solid but the start line is neutral instead of left enough
- **0 points** if it starts right of target or misses short because you did not cover the front

Good score:

- **12 or more out of 18**

That is enough to prove you are organizing the shot instead of just surviving it.

## Drill 2: The Extra-Club Carry Ladder

This drill teaches the lesson most golfers avoid: the lie changes the carry math.

Pick two yardages you actually face:

- one in the **120 to 140** range
- one in the **145 to 165** range

At each yardage:

- hit **3 balls with your normal club**
- hit **3 balls with one extra club**

Track only two things:

- which club clears the front more reliably
- which club leaves the calmer next putt

If the extra club keeps winning, stop acting surprised on the course and start using it sooner.

## The On-Course Version I Trust

When I get this lie during a real round, I use this quick script:

1. Aim left.
2. Check the front number.
3. Take the extra club if the carry is even a little annoying.
4. Swing at **80 to 85 percent**.
5. Hold the finish.

That is it.

No technical spiral.
No last-second rescue thoughts.

Just a lie-specific plan that respects what the slope is doing.

## Bottom Line

Ball-below-your-feet lies get easier the second you stop treating them like flat-ground shots.

Choke down a little.
Aim left on purpose.
Take more club when the front number matters.
Make a quieter swing and stay in posture.

Do that, and these shots stop being weird little scorecard ambushes and start becoming boring, manageable golf.

That is the whole point.
