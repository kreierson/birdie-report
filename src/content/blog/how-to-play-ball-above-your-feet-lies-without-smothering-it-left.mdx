Ball-above-your-feet lies are sneaky because they do not always feel terrifying.

They feel playable.
Friendly, even.

Then the shot starts tugging left, keeps turning, and suddenly your "pretty good swing" is walking into rough, bunker, trees, or a short-sided little personal crisis.

That is the trap.

This lie often does three things:

- makes the club play more upright
- helps the face turn over faster
- and tempts you to stand too close because the ball looks easier to reach

If the ugly opposite version is the one currently ruining your card, go straight to [how to play ball-below-your-feet lies without wiping it right](/blog/how-to-play-ball-below-your-feet-lies-without-wiping-it-right/). ## The Goal Is Not to Hold Off a Miracle Fade

On a ball-above-your-feet lie, I am not trying to manufacture some cute anti-slope shot.

I want:

- centered-enough contact
- the correct start line
- and a finish position that leaves me a boring next shot

That means respecting what the slope is already trying to do instead of fighting it like an idiot.

## My Default Setup for This Lie

This is my starting baseline:

- grip down **0.5 to 1 inch**
- stand a touch taller
- feel pressure through the **middle of your feet**
- let your arms hang without crowding yourself
- make peace with an **80 to 85 percent** swing

That setup does two jobs:

- it gives you room to return the middle of the face
- and it keeps you from throwing your hands at the ball late

Late hands are how the ball starts left of left.

## The First Rule: Move the Target Right Before You Even Think About Swinging

The lie already wants to help the ball draw more.

So stop aiming at the flag like this is flat turf.

My default start-line shift:

- **5 to 8 yards right** with wedges and short irons
- **8 to 12 yards right** with mid-irons
- more if the slope is severe and your stock shape already draws

That is the baseline.

Not the universal law.

But if you currently aim at the pin from this lie and act offended when the ball peels left, this adjustment alone fixes a stupid amount of damage.

If the pin is already tucked left, or the miss left is jail, use the same adult logic from [how to play front pins without making bogey](/blog/how-to-play-front-pins-without-making-bogey/), [how to play middle pins better](/blog/how-to-play-middle-pins-better/), and [stop short-siding yourself](/blog/stop-short-siding-yourself-approach-strategy/).

Hit the fat side.
Take your putt.
Shut up and enjoy the par chance.

## The Second Rule: Do Not Auto-Club Down

This is where golfers get weird.

They see:

- higher lie
- more upright delivery
- more draw

...and decide that must mean less club every time.

Nope.

Sometimes this lie does come out a little hotter.
Sometimes it launches a little easier.
That does **not** mean every ball-above-your-feet shot wants less loft or less club.

My default question is:

**Is the front of the green harmless enough that I can let the draw and flight do their thing?**

If yes:

- normal club is usually fine

If the slope is steep and there is room long:

- you can consider **one less club**

If the pin is front, the front is guarded, or the miss short is gross:

- keep the normal club
- favor the bigger target
- and stop trying to get clever

This lie is directional first.
Do not turn it into a distance-control problem too.

## The Third Rule: Back Up a Hair if You Feel Crowded

The quiet killer on this lie is setup crowding.

Because the ball sits higher, golfers slide too close without noticing.
Then the handle jams upward, the hands fire, and the face slams shut.

My quick fix:

- back up **about 1 inch**

Not a giant reset.
Not a full stance rebuild.

Just enough room that your arms can swing without feeling trapped.

If you feel jammed at address, you are probably already too close.

## The 4 Checkpoints I Want Before Pulling the Trigger

Before you hit the shot, answer these:

### 1. How severe is the slope really?

Mild slope and cartoon slope are not the same shot.

If it is only a little above your feet, the adjustment can stay modest.
If the ball looks like it is on a little shelf, give yourself more rightward start line and less pin-hunting ambition.

### 2. What is the expensive miss?

If left is dead:

- the pin is not your problem

If left is just rough and right is dead:

- you can be a little more aggressive

Cheap miss matters more than ego.

### 3. Do I have enough green for a draw-biased finish?

This is especially important on middle and back pins.

If the ball is likely to land and release a little left, I want enough putting surface to absorb that shape instead of pretending a tucked edge can still be the play.

### 4. Can I hold the finish for a full second?

If the answer is no, the swing is probably too aggressive for the lie.

That is the whole point of the **80 to 85 percent** cap.

## What Good Looks Like

From this lie, a good shot usually:

- starts right of the flag
- turns back toward the middle
- and finishes somewhere between the center and safe-left portion of the green

I am not asking for shot tracer porn.

I am asking for a miss pattern that does not create instant bogey work.

That matters even more from **125 to 175 yards**, where golfers see a playable number and immediately start pretending the hill is optional.

## Common Mistakes That Keep Showing Up

### Standing too close

This is the easiest way to create a smother-hook.

You crowd it.
The handle lifts.
The face turns over.
The ball leaves with bad intentions.

### Aiming at the flag anyway

This is not confidence.

It is denial wearing a polo.

The slope is already telling you the ball wants to work left.
Believe it.

### Swinging harder because the lie feels easy

This lie looks friendlier than ball-below-your-feet.
That makes golfers lazy.

Then they swing too hard, lose balance, and let the face rip shut through impact.

### Clubbing down into front trouble

If you take less club and the front edge is tight, you can turn a directional adjustment into a distance-control disaster too.

That is a greedy little double mistake.

## Drill 1: The 9-Ball Right-Window Drill

Use one sidehill station with the ball above your feet.

Hit:

- **3 balls with a wedge**
- **3 balls with an 8-iron**
- **3 balls with a 6- or 7-iron**

Pick a final target, then build a start-line window **right** of it.

Scoring:

- **2 points** if the ball starts in the right window and finishes on the green or safe side
- **1 point** if the contact is solid but the start line starts too neutral
- **0 points** if it starts left of target or over-curves into the danger side

Good score:

- **12 or more out of 18**

That is enough to prove you are organizing the shot instead of just hoping the hill behaves.

## Drill 2: The Crowd-Test Ladder

This drill tells you whether setup crowding is your actual problem.

Drop **6 balls** on the same lie.

Hit:

- **2 balls** from your normal address distance
- **2 balls** from **1 inch farther away**
- **2 balls** from **2 inches farther away**

Track only three things:

- start line
- curvature
- strike quality

If the one-inch-back version keeps winning, congratulations, you found the stupid little setup mistake that has been costing you greens.

## The On-Course Script I Trust

When I get this lie in a real round, I use this:

1. Grip down.
2. Stand a touch taller.
3. Aim right.
4. Keep the normal club unless the lie is severe and the target has room.
5. Swing at **80 to 85 percent** and hold the finish.

That is it.

No late-hand rescue move.
No weird anti-hook heroism.
No pretending the hill does not exist.

## Bottom Line

If ball-above-your-feet lies keep turning into surprise left misses, stop trying to solve them with talent.

Solve them with structure:

- grip down **0.5 to 1 inch**
- back up if you feel crowded
- move the start line **right**
- keep the swing at **80 to 85 percent**
- and only club down when the slope is steep **and** the target gives you room

That is how this lie stops feeling random.

And that is how it stops smothering good rounds for no reason.
